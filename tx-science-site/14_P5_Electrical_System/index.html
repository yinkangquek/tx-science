What Is Electricity?

Electricity is a form of energy that can be transferred from one place to another through conductors (materials that allow electricity to flow). Just like heat energy can warm our homes or mechanical energy can move objects, electrical energy can make things work.

Sources of Electricity

Batteries and Cells: These are portable sources of electrical energy. A battery (often called a cell in science) stores chemical energy and converts it into electrical energy when connected in a circuit. Common examples include the AA batteries in a torch or the 9V batteries in alarms.

Mains Electricity: This is the electricity supplied to our homes and schools through power lines. It comes from power stations where energy (from coal, water, wind, or solar) is converted into electrical energy and distributed through a network of cables.

Key Concept: Electricity is the flow of tiny particles called electrons through a material. These electrons are negatively charged and move from the negative terminal (-) of a battery through a circuit to the positive terminal (+).
Did You Know? Lightning is a form of electricity! During a thunderstorm, electrical energy builds up in clouds and is released as a massive electrical discharge that we see as lightning.

Components of an Electrical Circuit

To make electricity do useful work, we need to create a circuit. A circuit requires several key components to function properly:

1. Battery (or Cell)

The battery is the energy source of the circuit. It provides the electrical energy needed to make components work. A battery has two terminals: the positive terminal (+) marked on one end and the negative terminal (-) marked on the other. The difference in electrical potential between these terminals pushes electrons through the circuit.

  • Single Cell: A single battery unit (like a AA battery)
  • Battery Pack: Multiple cells connected together to provide more electrical energy

2. Wires (Conductors)

Wires are made from metals like copper that allow electricity to flow easily. Wires connect the battery to other components and create the path for electricity. The wires have an insulating coating (usually plastic) to prevent electricity from flowing where it shouldn't and to protect us from electric shock.

3. Switch

A switch controls whether the circuit is complete or broken. When the switch is closed (ON), the circuit is complete and electricity can flow. When the switch is open (OFF), the circuit is broken and electricity cannot flow. Without a switch, you wouldn't be able to turn electrical devices on and off.

4. Load (Bulb, Buzzer, Motor, or Other Device)

The load is the component that uses electrical energy to do something useful:

  • Bulb (Light Bulb): Converts electrical energy into light and heat energy
  • Buzzer: Converts electrical energy into sound energy
  • Motor: Converts electrical energy into mechanical energy (movement)
  • Heater: Converts electrical energy into heat energy

Open and Closed Circuits

Closed Circuit (Complete Path)

A closed circuit is a complete, unbroken path through which electricity can flow. When a circuit is closed:

  • All components are properly connected
  • The switch is ON (closed)
  • Electricity flows from the battery through the circuit and back to the battery
  • Components like bulbs light up, buzzers make sound, and motors spin

Open Circuit (Broken Path)

An open circuit is a broken path through which electricity cannot flow. When a circuit is open:

  • There is a break or gap in the circuit path
  • The switch is OFF (open)
  • Electricity cannot flow from the battery
  • Components like bulbs do not light up
Key Concept: A complete circuit is like a complete circle or loop. Electricity must be able to flow all the way around and back to where it started. If there's any break in the loop, the electricity cannot flow.
Did You Know? When you flip a light switch at home, you're either opening or closing a circuit. The switch breaks or completes the path between the battery/power source and the light bulb.

Conductors and insulators work together in every electrical device. The conductor carries electricity where we want it, while the insulator protects us by blocking it from other places!

Conductors and Insulators

What Are Conductors?

Conductors are materials that allow electricity to flow through them easily. Most metals are good conductors of electricity. The electrons in these materials can move freely, allowing electrical energy to pass through.

Common Conductors:

  • Copper: Most common in wires due to excellent conductivity and affordability
  • Silver: Even better conductor than copper but more expensive
  • Aluminum: Good conductor, used in some electrical applications
  • Water: Pure water is a poor conductor, but water with dissolved minerals conducts electricity
  • Graphite: A form of carbon that conducts electricity

What Are Insulators?

Insulators are materials that do NOT allow electricity to flow through them easily. The electrons in these materials are tightly bound and cannot move freely. Insulators are used to protect us from electrical shock and to direct electricity along the desired path.

Common Insulators:

  • Plastic: Used to coat wires and cables
  • Rubber: Used in electric plugs and flexible cables
  • Wood: Used in telegraph poles and some electrical equipment
  • Glass: Used in insulators for power lines
  • Ceramic: Used in electrical insulators
  • Air: Acts as an insulator under normal conditions

Why Wires Have Plastic Coating

Wires used in circuits have a copper or metal core (the conductor) surrounded by a plastic or rubber coating (the insulator). This design serves two purposes:

  1. Safety: The insulating coating prevents electricity from flowing through you if you touch the wire. This protects you from electric shock.
  2. Control: The coating directs electricity to flow only through the conductor wire and prevents it from jumping to other nearby conductors.
Key Concept: Conductors let electricity through; insulators block electricity. Every electrical device uses both: the conductor (like copper wire) carries electricity, while the insulator (like plastic coating) protects us from it.

⚡ Simple Complete Circuit

For electricity to flow and the bulb to light up, we need a complete circuit: a power source (battery), a conductor (wire), a load (bulb), and a closed switch!

Series Circuits

What Is a Series Circuit?

A series circuit is a circuit where all components are connected in a single path, one after another. Electricity flows from the battery through the first component, then the second, then the third, and so on, before returning to the battery. There is only ONE path for electricity to follow.

Properties of Series Circuits

1. Single Path: Electricity has only one route through the circuit. All components are in line with each other.

2. Same Current Everywhere: The same electric current flows through all components. The current doesn't split or divide; it's the same everywhere in the series circuit.

3. Brightness Decreases With More Bulbs: If you add more bulbs to a series circuit, each bulb will be dimmer than before. This is because the electrical energy from the battery is divided among all the bulbs. More bulbs = less energy for each bulb = dimmer light.

4. One Failure Breaks the Whole Circuit: This is a major property of series circuits. If ANY component breaks or is removed, the entire circuit stops working. If one bulb blows out, all the other bulbs go dark because the circuit is broken.

Key Concept: Series circuits are like a chain: if one link breaks, the entire chain is broken. All components must work for the circuit to work.
⚡ Series vs Parallel Circuits

SERIES CIRCUIT

PARALLEL CIRCUIT

Series circuits have one path, so one failure stops everything. Parallel circuits have multiple paths, so each component works independently. Your home uses parallel circuits!

Parallel Circuits

What Is a Parallel Circuit?

A parallel circuit is a circuit where components are connected in SEPARATE branches. Each component has its own path from the battery. The electricity can split and flow through different branches simultaneously, then rejoin before returning to the battery. There are MULTIPLE paths for electricity to follow.

Properties of Parallel Circuits

1. Multiple Paths: Electricity can flow through several different routes. If one branch is blocked, electricity can still flow through the other branches.

2. Different Currents in Different Branches: The total current from the battery splits among the branches. Each branch carries some of the current, but the total current returning to the battery equals the sum of all branch currents.

3. Brightness Remains Constant: In a parallel circuit, each bulb receives the full voltage from the battery. Therefore, all bulbs shine with the same brightness regardless of how many bulbs are connected. Adding more bulbs doesn't make the existing bulbs dimmer.

4. One Failure Does NOT Break the Whole Circuit: This is the major advantage of parallel circuits. If one bulb blows out, the circuit in that branch is broken, BUT electricity can still flow through the other branches. The other bulbs continue to work normally.

Key Concept: Parallel circuits are like separate paths: if one path is blocked, you can still travel through the other paths. All branches work independently of each other.
Did You Know? The electricity in your home is wired in parallel! This is why you can turn off one light without affecting others, and why multiple appliances can run at the same time from different outlets.

When to Use Each Type

Series Circuits: Series circuits are useful when you need:

  • Simple circuits with few components
  • All components to be controlled together
  • A way to make sure current cannot exceed a certain level (using a series resistor)

Parallel Circuits: Parallel circuits are preferred when you need:

  • Multiple components to work independently
  • Each component to maintain full brightness or function
  • Failure of one component not to affect others (like household wiring)
  • Flexibility to add or remove components easily

Electrical Safety

Electricity is very useful but also very dangerous if not handled properly. Always remember these safety rules:

  • Never play with power outlets or electrical wires
  • Never stick objects into electrical sockets
  • Keep water away from electrical appliances and sockets
  • Always ask an adult before using electrical equipment
  • If someone is electrocuted, DO NOT touch them. Call for help immediately
  • Never touch downed power lines
  • Be careful with electrical cords - don't pull them or damage them
  • Use properly grounded electrical equipment with three-pin plugs in wet areas

PSLE-Style Practice Questions

Section A: Multiple Choice Questions (1 mark each)

Question 1: Circuit Components

Which of the following is NOT a component of a simple electrical circuit?

Correct Answer: B) Water
Water is not a component of an electrical circuit. A simple circuit needs: battery (energy source), wire (conductor), switch (control), and a load like a bulb.
Question 2: Open vs Closed Circuit

When the switch in a circuit is ON, the circuit is said to be:

Correct Answer: B) Closed
When the switch is ON, it completes the circuit path, making it a closed circuit. Electricity can flow through the complete path and reach the load.
Question 3: Conductors

Which of the following is the BEST conductor of electricity?

Correct Answer: C) Copper
Copper is a metal and an excellent conductor of electricity. It's commonly used in wires. Rubber, plastic, and wood are all insulators.
Question 4: Series Circuit Property

In a series circuit, if one bulb blows out:

Correct Answer: C) All bulbs go out
In a series circuit, there is only one path. If one bulb breaks, the circuit is broken and electricity cannot flow, so all bulbs go out.
Question 5: Parallel Circuit Advantage

Which statement about parallel circuits is TRUE?

Correct Answer: C) Each component has its own separate path
In a parallel circuit, components are connected in separate branches. This is why one component can fail without affecting others, and why household wiring uses parallel circuits.

Section B: Open-Ended Questions (2-3 marks each)

Question 6: Explaining Open Circuits

Explain why a light bulb does not light up when there is a break in the wire connecting it to the battery.

Model Answer:
A light bulb needs electricity to flow through it to light up. The electricity flows in a closed circuit (complete path). When there is a break in the wire, the circuit becomes open (incomplete). Without a complete path, electricity cannot flow from the battery to the bulb. Therefore, the bulb does not light up.
Question 7: Series Circuit Problem

In a circuit, Bulb A and Bulb B are connected in series. If Bulb A blows, what will happen to Bulb B? Explain your answer.

Model Answer:
Bulb B will go out. In a series circuit, there is only one path for electricity. All components are connected one after another. If Bulb A blows, it creates a break in this single path. This stops electricity from flowing through the entire circuit. Since electricity cannot reach Bulb B, it will not light up (it will go out).
Question 8: Parallel Circuit Advantage

Explain why parallel circuits are better than series circuits for household electrical wiring.

Model Answer:
Parallel circuits are better for household wiring because: (1) Each appliance has its own separate branch, so you can control each one independently. You can turn off one light without affecting others. (2) If one appliance fails, the others continue to work because electricity can still flow through other branches. (3) Each appliance receives the full voltage from the power source, so they all work at full strength. In a series circuit, if one appliance breaks, all stop working, which is inconvenient for a home.
