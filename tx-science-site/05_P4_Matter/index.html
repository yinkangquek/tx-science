📚 Interactive Notes

What Is Matter?

Everything around us is made of matter. Matter is anything that has mass and occupies space. In other words, if something takes up room and has weight, it is matter.

The key properties of matter are:

  • Mass: The amount of material in an object. We measure mass in grams or kilograms.
  • Occupies Space (Volume): Matter takes up room. Every object you can see and touch occupies space.

💡 Key Concept

Even air is matter! You cannot see air, but it has mass and occupies space. Here's proof: A deflated balloon is light and takes up little space. When you inflate the balloon with air, it becomes heavier and takes up much more space. The air inside has mass and volume!

The Three States of Matter

All matter exists in one of three states: solid, liquid, or gas. The state depends on how the particles are arranged and how much they move.

🔬 Three States of Matter - Particle Arrangement
🔄 Changes of State - The Cycle

↑ Add heat to move up the cycle (melting, evaporation) | ↓ Remove heat to move down (freezing, condensation)

1. Solids

Properties of Solids:

  • Fixed shape: A solid keeps the same shape no matter what container it is in. A block of wood is always block-shaped.
  • Fixed volume: The amount of space a solid takes up does not change.
  • Cannot be compressed: You cannot squeeze a solid into a smaller space.
  • Particles are tightly packed: In a solid, particles are very close together and vibrate in place but do not move around freely.

Examples: ice, wood, metal, pencil, book, rock, glass

2. Liquids

Properties of Liquids:

  • No fixed shape: A liquid takes the shape of the container it is in. Water in a cup is cup-shaped, but water in a bowl is bowl-shaped.
  • Fixed volume: The amount of space a liquid takes up stays the same.
  • Cannot be easily compressed: It is very difficult to squeeze a liquid into a smaller space.
  • Particles are close but can slide: In a liquid, particles are still close together, but they can slide past each other, allowing the liquid to flow.

Examples: water, milk, juice, oil, honey, blood

3. Gases

Properties of Gases:

  • No fixed shape: A gas spreads out to fill whatever space is available. It takes the shape of the entire container, not just part of it.
  • No fixed volume: A gas can expand or shrink depending on the container size.
  • Can be compressed: You can squeeze a gas into a smaller space. For example, pumping air into a bicycle tire compresses the air.
  • Particles are far apart and move freely: In a gas, particles are spread out and move very quickly in all directions.

Examples: air (oxygen, nitrogen), carbon dioxide, steam, hydrogen, helium (in balloons)

Comparing the Three States of Matter

Property Solid Liquid Gas

🔬 Did You Know?

The densest state of matter is a solid, followed by a liquid, and then a gas. This is why a solid sinks in water (it's denser), and air bubbles rise up (gas is less dense than liquid).

The Particle Model of Matter

All matter is made up of tiny particles called atoms and molecules. These particles are always moving, but the amount of space between them and how fast they move differs depending on the state.

In a Solid: Particles are packed tightly with very little space between them. They vibrate in place but stay in fixed positions. This is why solids have a fixed shape and volume.

In a Liquid: Particles are still close together, but they have more space between them than in a solid. They can slide past each other, which allows the liquid to flow and take the shape of its container.

In a Gas: Particles are far apart with lots of space between them. They move very quickly and randomly in all directions. This is why gases can fill an entire container and be compressed.

Changes of State

Matter can change from one state to another when heat is added or removed. Let's explore each type of change:

1. Melting (Solid → Liquid)

Definition: Melting is when a solid changes into a liquid by adding heat.

What happens: When you add heat to a solid, the particles vibrate faster and faster. Eventually, they vibrate so much that they break free from their fixed positions and can slide past each other. The solid becomes a liquid.

Example: Ice (solid) melts into water (liquid) when it is heated. At 0°C (32°F), ice melts into water.

Real-life example: A chocolate bar left in the sun starts to melt because the heat from the sun gives energy to the particles.

2. Freezing (Liquid → Solid)

Definition: Freezing is when a liquid changes into a solid by removing heat.

What happens: When you cool a liquid, the particles slow down and vibrate less. Eventually, they move so slowly that they form fixed positions and become a solid.

Example: Water (liquid) freezes into ice (solid) when it is cooled. At 0°C (32°F), water freezes into ice.

Real-life example: Water in a freezer turns into ice cubes because the freezer removes heat from the water.

💡 Key Concept

Melting and Freezing are Opposite Processes: Melting adds heat and turns a solid into a liquid. Freezing removes heat and turns a liquid into a solid. They happen at the same temperature but in opposite directions.

3. Boiling & Evaporation (Liquid → Gas)

Definition: Boiling and evaporation are when a liquid changes into a gas by adding heat.

What happens: When you add enough heat to a liquid, the particles gain so much energy that they escape from the surface and become a gas. The liquid turns into a gas.

Boiling:

  • Boiling happens at a specific temperature (100°C for water).
  • Bubbles form inside the liquid and rise to the surface.
  • The entire liquid turns into a gas throughout its volume.
  • Example: Heating water on a stove until it boils and steam rises.

Evaporation:

  • Evaporation can happen at any temperature, not just at boiling point.
  • Particles escape from the surface of the liquid.
  • Only particles at the surface turn into a gas.
  • Example: Wet clothes hanging on a clothesline eventually dry because water evaporates from the surface.

Real-life example: A kettle of water boils at 100°C and turns into steam (gas). But a puddle on the ground also becomes a gas through evaporation, even though the temperature is much lower.

❓ Think About It

Why does a wet towel eventually dry even if you don't heat it? The water evaporates from the towel at room temperature, turning into water vapor in the air. This is why evaporation happens much slower than boiling, but it still happens!

4. Condensation (Gas → Liquid)

Definition: Condensation is when a gas changes into a liquid by removing heat.

What happens: When you cool a gas, the particles slow down and lose energy. Eventually, they come close enough together to form a liquid.

Example: Steam (gas) condenses into water (liquid) when it cools. This is the opposite of boiling.

Real-life examples:

  • Water droplets on a mirror or window after a hot shower - steam from the hot water condenses on the cold glass.
  • Water droplets on the outside of a cold glass of water - water vapor in the air condenses on the cold surface.
  • Morning dew on grass - water vapor in the air condenses on the cold grass overnight.

💡 Key Concept

Condensation is Opposite to Boiling/Evaporation: When a gas is cooled, it condenses into a liquid. This happens at the same temperature as boiling, but in the opposite direction.

⭐ Sublimation: Solid Directly to Gas ▼

Some solids can change directly into a gas without becoming a liquid first. This is called sublimation.

Example: Dry ice (solid carbon dioxide) sublimes into carbon dioxide gas without melting into a liquid first. This is why dry ice seems to "disappear" and creates fog.

Another example: Ice can sublime in very cold, dry places without melting.

📝 PSLE-Style Practice Questions

Section A: Multiple Choice Questions (5 marks)

Choose the best answer for each question.

Question 1
Mei has a glass of water sitting on the table. After some time, some of the water evaporates. Which statement best describes what happens to the water during evaporation?
A) The water turns into ice
B) The water changes from liquid into gas
C) The water becomes more solid
D) The water's mass increases
✓ Correct Answer: B

Explanation: Evaporation is the process where a liquid changes into a gas. Water from the glass escapes as water vapor into the air. This is different from boiling because evaporation happens at room temperature without bubbles forming.

Question 2
Which of the following statements about solids is correct?
A) Solids can take the shape of any container
B) Solids have fixed volume but no fixed shape
C) Solids have both fixed shape and fixed volume
D) Solids can be easily compressed
✓ Correct Answer: C

Explanation: Solids have both fixed shape and fixed volume because their particles are tightly packed in fixed positions. Liquids have fixed volume but no fixed shape. Gases have neither fixed shape nor fixed volume.

Question 3
The diagram shows a glass of hot water covered with a lid. After 20 minutes, water droplets form on the inside of the lid. Why do these water droplets form?
A) The hot water is freezing
B) Water vapor rises and condenses on the cold lid
C) Ice is melting on top of the water
D) The water is subliming
✓ Correct Answer: B

Explanation: When water is heated, it evaporates and becomes water vapor (gas). This vapor rises and touches the cold lid. The cold surface causes the water vapor to condense back into liquid water droplets. This is the process of condensation.

Question 4
Which of the following statements about gases is true?
A) Gases have fixed volume and can be compressed
B) Gases have no fixed shape and no fixed volume
C) Gases cannot be compressed because their particles are tightly packed
D) Gases have fixed shape like solids
✓ Correct Answer: B

Explanation: Gases have no fixed shape (they fill their container) and no fixed volume (they can expand or shrink). Gas particles are far apart and move freely, which is why gases can be compressed.

Question 5
Ice turns into water when heated. What is the name of this change of state?
A) Freezing
B) Evaporation
C) Melting
D) Condensation
✓ Correct Answer: C

Explanation: Melting is when a solid (ice) changes into a liquid (water) by adding heat. The opposite process is freezing, where a liquid becomes a solid.

Section B: Open-Ended Questions (6 marks)

Answer the following questions in complete sentences.

Question 6 (2 marks)
Mei placed a glass of cold water on the table. After 10 minutes, she noticed water droplets on the outside of the glass. Explain how the water droplets formed on the outside of the glass.
✓ Model Answer

Water vapor in the air around the glass comes into contact with the cold surface of the glass. The cold temperature causes the water vapor (gas) to condense into liquid water droplets. This is the process of condensation.

Key points: Mention water vapor from air, cold glass surface, and condensation process.

Question 7 (2 marks)
Describe two differences between the properties of liquids and gases.
✓ Model Answer

Difference 1: Shape - Liquids do not have a fixed shape but take the shape of the container, while gases also do not have a fixed shape but fill the entire container.

Difference 2: Volume - Liquids have a fixed volume, while gases do not have a fixed volume and can expand or contract depending on the container size.

Alternative: Liquids cannot be easily compressed, but gases can be compressed.

Question 8 (2 marks)
Explain what happens to the particles of a substance when it is heated and changes from a solid to a liquid.
✓ Model Answer

When heat is added to a solid, the particles gain energy and vibrate faster. The particles vibrate so much that they break free from their fixed positions and can slide past each other. This allows the solid to become a liquid.

Key points: Mention particles vibrating, gaining energy, breaking free from fixed positions, and sliding past each other.
