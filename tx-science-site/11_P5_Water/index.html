Water

Water Around Us

Water is one of the most important substances on Earth. It covers about 71% of our planet and is essential for all living things. But where exactly is all this water found?

Where Is Water Found?

Water exists in many different places in our environment:

  • Oceans: The vast oceans contain about 97% of all water on Earth. This water is salty and covers most of our planet.
  • Rivers and Lakes: These bodies of fresh water flow across continents and provide water for many plants and animals.
  • Ice Caps and Glaciers: Large amounts of frozen water are locked up at the North and South Poles and on mountain peaks.
  • Underground Water: Beneath the soil and rocks, there are vast underground reserves of water (groundwater) that we use through wells.
  • The Atmosphere: Water exists as invisible water vapour in the air around us. We cannot see it, but it is always there.
  • Living Things: All plants and animals contain water. About 60% of your body is water!
  • Soil: Water is held in the spaces between soil particles and helps plants grow.
Did You Know?

If you could collect all the water on Earth into one giant container, it would have the same amount of water today as it did 2,000 years ago! Water is constantly moving and changing form, but the total amount stays the same.

Water in Our Daily Lives

We use water every single day in many different ways:

  • Drinking water to stay healthy
  • Cooking our food
  • Washing ourselves and our clothes
  • Growing crops and plants
  • Generating electricity at power stations
  • Manufacturing products in factories
  • Recreation and transport
Key Concept:

Water is a renewable resource, which means it can be used again and again as it moves through the water cycle. However, fresh water that we can drink is not unlimited, so we must use it wisely.

The Water Cycle

The water cycle, also called the hydrological cycle, is the continuous movement of water on, above, and below Earth's surface. It is one of the most important natural processes on our planet. The water cycle involves four main stages:

💧 The Water Cycle

The water cycle is a continuous process: water evaporates from oceans, condenses into clouds, falls as rain on land, and flows back to the ocean!

Stage 1: Evaporation

What happens: The sun's heat warms water in oceans, rivers, lakes, and other bodies of water. This heat energy causes the water molecules to move very fast. Eventually, some water molecules have enough energy to escape from the liquid water and become water vapour (a gas).

Where it happens:

  • Oceans (the biggest source)
  • Lakes and rivers
  • Soil (moisture in the ground)
  • Plants through their leaves (transpiration)

Key point: Evaporation is when water changes from a liquid state to a gas state. You cannot see the water after it evaporates because it becomes invisible water vapour in the air.

Think About It:

Have you noticed how puddles disappear after rain on a sunny day? That is evaporation happening right before your eyes! The water does not disappear—it just changes form and rises into the air.

Stage 2: Condensation

What happens: As water vapour rises higher and higher into the atmosphere, it moves away from the warm sun. The temperature gets colder and colder. When water vapour cools down enough, it changes back into tiny liquid water droplets. This process is called condensation.

Where it happens:

  • High in the atmosphere where the air is cold
  • Around microscopic particles of dust and salt in the air
  • The water droplets gather together to form clouds

Key point: Condensation is when water changes from a gas state back to a liquid state. The millions of tiny water droplets together make the clouds we see in the sky.

You experience condensation every day:

  • When you breathe out on a cold window and it fogs up
  • When water droplets form on the outside of a cold drink on a hot day
  • When morning dew forms on grass

Stage 3: Precipitation

What happens: Clouds contain millions and millions of water droplets. As more water vapour condenses, the cloud gets heavier and heavier. Eventually, the water droplets combine together and become too heavy to float in the air anymore. They fall back to Earth as precipitation.

Forms of precipitation:

  • Rain: The most common form. Water falls as liquid drops.
  • Snow: When it is very cold, water freezes into crystals and falls as snow.
  • Hail: In strong storms, ice balls form and fall.
  • Sleet: A mixture of rain and snow.

Key point: Precipitation is water falling from clouds back to Earth's surface. In Singapore, we mostly experience rain because of our tropical climate.

Did You Know?

A single cloud can contain millions of water droplets, and each droplet is so small that about 1 million of them would fit on the head of a pin! Yet when they all fall together as rain, they can fill rivers and reservoirs.

Stage 4: Collection

What happens: When precipitation falls to Earth, the water collects in different places:

  • In Oceans: Most rainfall falls directly into the ocean or eventually flows to the ocean.
  • In Rivers and Lakes: Rainwater flows downhill, forming streams that join together to make rivers. Some water collects in lakes.
  • In the Soil: Some water soaks into the ground and becomes groundwater, which plants use and which refills underground reserves.
  • On Ice: In very cold regions, some water remains frozen as ice and snow.

Key point: Collection is where the water cycle begins again! The water in oceans, rivers, lakes, and soil is warmed by the sun and begins to evaporate, and the whole cycle starts over.

The Complete Water Cycle

Key Concept: The Complete Cycle

The water cycle is continuous and never stops. The same water has been cycling through evaporation, condensation, precipitation, and collection for billions of years. This water you drink today might have been drunk by dinosaurs millions of years ago!

💧 Water Properties

Water can exist in three states depending on temperature. As a liquid, water is unique because it can dissolve more substances than any other liquid!

Water in Three States

Water is special because it can exist in three different states of matter. You learned about states of matter in your P4 Science topic on Matter. Let's see how water demonstrates all three states:

Ice (Solid State)

What it is: When water is frozen, it becomes ice. Ice is a solid, which means:

  • It has a definite shape (it does not flow or change shape easily)
  • It has a definite volume (it takes up a specific amount of space)
  • The water molecules are tightly packed together and move very slowly

Where you find it in the water cycle: In the form of snow and hail that falls during precipitation, and in ice caps and glaciers at the poles.

Temperature: Ice forms when water is cooled to 0°C (32°F) or below.

Water (Liquid State)

What it is: The water you drink, the water in the ocean, and the water in lakes are all liquid water. A liquid:

  • Does not have a definite shape (it takes the shape of its container)
  • Has a definite volume (it takes up a specific amount of space)
  • The water molecules are loosely packed and can move around freely

Where you find it in the water cycle: In oceans, rivers, lakes, soil, and as rainwater falling from clouds.

Temperature: Liquid water normally exists between 0°C and 100°C.

Water Vapour (Gas State)

What it is: Water vapour is invisible water in the form of a gas. A gas:

  • Does not have a definite shape (it spreads out to fill any space)
  • Does not have a definite volume (it expands infinitely)
  • The water molecules are far apart and move very quickly and randomly

Where you find it in the water cycle: In the atmosphere, formed during evaporation. You cannot see it because it is invisible, but it is always there!

Temperature: Water vapour can form at any temperature through evaporation. At 100°C, rapid boiling occurs, but evaporation happens at much lower temperatures — which is how puddles dry up and oceans feed the water cycle.

Key Concept: State Changes in the Water Cycle

Melting: Ice (solid) → Water (liquid) — happens when ice is heated
Freezing: Water (liquid) → Ice (solid) — happens when water is cooled
Evaporation: Water (liquid) → Water vapour (gas) — happens when water is heated by the sun
Condensation: Water vapour (gas) → Water (liquid) — happens when water vapour cools down
Sublimation: Ice (solid) → Water vapour (gas) — happens when ice is heated directly without melting

Did You Know?

Water vapour is always in the air around you! On a humid day (when it feels sticky), there is more water vapour in the air. On a dry day, there is less. But it is always there, even when you cannot see it.

Why Is the Water Cycle Important?

The water cycle is one of the most important natural processes on Earth. Here's why:

1. Provides Fresh Water

The water cycle is nature's way of purifying and distributing fresh water. When salt water from the ocean evaporates, the salt is left behind. The water vapour that rises into the atmosphere is pure, fresh water. When this water falls as precipitation, it provides fresh water for us to drink, use, and to water our crops. Without the water cycle, we would have no fresh water!

2. Supports All Life

Every living thing on Earth depends on water to survive. The water cycle ensures that water reaches all corners of our planet:

  • Plants absorb groundwater through their roots and use it to grow. Some water evaporates from plant leaves through transpiration (evapotranspiration).
  • Animals need water to drink and for their bodies to function. Many animals live in water or depend on water sources.
  • Entire ecosystems depend on rivers, lakes, and wetlands that are fed by the water cycle.

Without the water cycle, there would be no life on Earth!

3. Regulates Earth's Climate

The water cycle is crucial for controlling Earth's climate and temperature:

  • Evaporation cools the surface: When water evaporates from oceans and land, it removes heat energy. This helps cool the planet.
  • Water vapour traps heat: Water vapour in the atmosphere is a greenhouse gas that helps keep heat in our atmosphere. Without this, Earth would be too cold.
  • Clouds reflect sunlight: Clouds in the atmosphere reflect some of the sun's radiation back into space, which helps regulate temperature.
  • Precipitation brings moisture: The water cycle distributes moisture around the globe, affecting weather patterns and climate.

The water cycle is like Earth's natural air conditioning system, keeping our planet at the right temperature for life!

4. Cycles Nutrients

Water transports essential nutrients through the environment:

  • Water dissolves minerals from rocks and soil
  • These nutrients flow into rivers and oceans
  • They feed aquatic plants and animals
  • When organisms die, water helps return nutrients to the soil for new plants to use
Think About It:

The water cycle is so powerful and important that it has been running continuously for about 4 billion years! Every drop of water on Earth is part of this amazing cycle, and it has been part of countless living things throughout history.

Water Conservation

While the total amount of water on Earth stays the same, the amount of fresh water that we can use is limited. In Singapore, we must be especially careful about water conservation because we have limited natural water resources.

Singapore's Water Story: The 4 National Taps

Singapore's water comes from four sources, which we call the 4 National Taps:

  • 1st Tap - Rainfall: Singapore collects water from rain in reservoirs. This is our most natural source.
  • 2nd Tap - Water from Malaysia: We import treated water through pipelines from Johor, Malaysia. This is an important part of our water supply.
  • 3rd Tap - Recycled Water (NEWater): We treat used water and recycle it for use in industry and homes. NEWater is a great example of water conservation!
  • 4th Tap - Desalination: We remove salt from seawater to make it fresh and drinkable. This is new technology that helps us become more independent.

Because we depend on these sources, every person in Singapore must help conserve water.

Why Must We Conserve Water?

Limited fresh water: Although 71% of Earth is covered by water, 97% of it is salty ocean water. Only about 3% is fresh water, and much of that is frozen in ice caps. The fresh water we can actually use is much less than 1% of all Earth's water.

Growing population: As more people live in our cities, we use more water every day. Our water supply must stretch to serve everyone.

Climate change: Droughts and changing weather patterns make it harder to collect rainfall. We cannot depend on rain being consistent.

Environmental protection: Rivers and wetlands need water to support plants and animals. If we use too much water, these ecosystems suffer.

Key Concept:

Water is a precious resource, and each of us has a responsibility to use it wisely. Even small changes in our daily habits can add up to make a big difference!

How Can You Conserve Water? Simple Habits to Save Water

In the Bathroom:

  • Take shorter showers (aim for 5 minutes or less)
  • Turn off the tap while brushing your teeth
  • Fix leaking taps immediately—a single dripping tap can waste thousands of litres per year!
  • Use a bucket to rinse after shampooing

In the Kitchen:

  • Turn off the tap while washing dishes or vegetables
  • Collect water while waiting for hot water, and use it to water plants
  • Run the dishwasher only when it is full
  • Keep a jug of drinking water in the refrigerator instead of running the tap

Around Your Home:

  • Water plants early in the morning or evening to reduce evaporation loss
  • Use a watering can instead of a hose
  • Check for and fix leaks in pipes
  • Use a broom to sweep rather than spraying water with a hose

At School and Community:

  • Report leaking taps to your teachers or maintenance staff
  • Use only the water you need
  • Encourage friends and family to save water
  • Participate in water conservation campaigns
Did You Know?

Every person in Singapore uses about 148 litres of water per day on average. That's like filling a large bathtub every single day! By reducing our daily use to 130 litres per person, we can save enough water to supply thousands more people.

PSLE-Style Practice Questions

Section A: Multiple Choice Questions (1 mark each)

Choose the correct answer for each question.

Question 1
Which of the following is an example of evaporation in the water cycle?
Correct Answer: B) A puddle disappearing on a sunny day

Evaporation is when liquid water changes to water vapour (gas) due to heat from the sun. A puddle disappearing shows water evaporating.

Question 2
What is the correct order of stages in the water cycle?
Correct Answer: B) Evaporation → Condensation → Precipitation → Collection

The water cycle starts with evaporation (sun heats water), followed by condensation (water vapour cools into droplets forming clouds), precipitation (water falls as rain), and collection (water gathers in bodies of water).

Question 3
Which state of water is present in the atmosphere as water vapour?
Correct Answer: C) Gas

Water vapour is water in its gaseous state. It is invisible and forms when liquid water evaporates due to the sun's heat.

Question 4
Why is the water cycle important for life on Earth?
Correct Answer: B) It distributes fresh water and regulates Earth's climate

The water cycle is crucial because it provides fresh water for all living things and helps regulate Earth's temperature through evaporation, cloud formation, and precipitation.

Question 5
Which of the following is NOT one of Singapore's 4 National Taps for water?
Correct Answer: B) Water imported from Iceland

Singapore's 4 National Taps are: (1) Rainfall, (2) Imported water from Malaysia, (3) Recycled water (NEWater), and (4) Desalinated water. Water is not imported from Iceland.

Section B: Open-Ended Questions (3-5 marks each)

Answer these questions in complete sentences. Model answers are provided.

Question 6
On a hot day, water puddles on the road disappear after a few hours. Explain where the water went using your knowledge of the water cycle. (3 marks)
Model Answer:

The water in the puddles evaporated. The sun's heat warmed the water and gave the water molecules enough energy to escape from the liquid water and become water vapour (a gas). The water vapour rose into the atmosphere and is now part of the air around us. Although we cannot see the water anymore, it has not disappeared—it has just changed form. (3 marks: 1 for naming evaporation, 1 for explaining that sun's heat caused it, 1 for mentioning change to water vapour/gas)

Question 7
Describe the process of condensation in the water cycle and explain why it is essential for forming clouds. (4 marks)
Model Answer:

Condensation is the process where water vapour cools and changes back into tiny liquid water droplets. As water vapour rises high into the atmosphere where the air is colder, it loses heat energy. When the temperature drops enough, the water vapour condenses around microscopic particles of dust and salt in the air. These water droplets are so tiny and light that they float in the air together to form visible clouds. Without condensation, the water vapour would stay as an invisible gas and clouds would not form. (4 marks: 1 for defining condensation, 1 for explaining cooling and change to liquid, 1 for mentioning condensation around particles, 1 for explaining why it is essential for clouds)

Question 8
Water is often described as a "renewable resource," but Singapore still needs to conserve water carefully. Explain this apparent contradiction and suggest three practical ways you can help conserve water at home. (5 marks)
Model Answer:

Although water is renewable because it cycles continuously through the water cycle and the total amount on Earth remains constant, fresh water is limited. Most water on Earth is salt water in oceans, and the fresh water we can use is less than 1% of all water. Additionally, the water cycle takes time, and our population is growing faster than the water cycle can supply fresh water. Therefore, Singapore must conserve water to ensure everyone has enough.

Three ways to conserve water at home:
1) Turn off the tap while brushing teeth or soaping hands to prevent water from running unnecessarily.
2) Take shorter showers (5 minutes or less) instead of long showers to reduce water usage.
3) Fix any leaking taps immediately, as a single dripping tap can waste thousands of litres per year.

(5 marks: 1 for explaining renewable vs. limited fresh water, 1 for explaining why conservation is still needed, 3 for suggesting three practical ways with clear explanations)

Values: Environmental Responsibility and Water Stewardship

Environmental Responsibility

Taking care of our environment by understanding how natural systems like the water cycle work and protecting them for future generations.

Water Stewardship

Using water wisely and conserving this precious resource. Every action counts—from turning off taps to taking shorter showers.

Respect for Nature

Appreciating the amazing processes in nature, like the water cycle, that make life on Earth possible.

Personal Action

Understanding that individual choices matter. Your water conservation habits can inspire others and contribute to a sustainable future.
