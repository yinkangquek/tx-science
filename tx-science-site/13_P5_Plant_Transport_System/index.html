Understanding Plant Transport

How Do Plants Get Water?

Plants need water to survive and grow. But where do they get their water from? The answer lies in the roots.

Deep underground, plant roots spread out into the soil. The roots have special structures called root hairs—these are tiny, hair-like extensions that stick out from the root. Root hairs have a very large surface area, which allows them to absorb lots of water and dissolved minerals from the soil.

Think of root hairs like tiny sponges. When soil is wet, the root hairs soak up water and minerals (like nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium). These minerals are essential nutrients that help the plant grow strong and healthy. The water and minerals are absorbed through the root hair cell walls and move into the rest of the plant.

Key Concept: Root hairs absorb water and minerals from the soil through their large surface area.
Did You Know? A single plant root can have millions of root hairs. If you could somehow unroll all the root hairs from a small plant, they could stretch for many metres!

Transport in the Stem

Once water is absorbed by the roots, it needs to travel all the way up to the leaves—sometimes several metres high! But how does the water move against gravity?

The secret is hidden inside the stem. If you cut open a plant stem and look closely (or use a magnifying glass), you will see tiny tubes running through it. These tubes are called xylem. The xylem is like a network of straws inside the plant.

🌱 Xylem and Phloem Transport

The stem has two transport systems working together. Xylem moves water up, while phloem moves food down throughout the plant!

Water and minerals absorbed by the roots move up through the xylem tubes in the stem. This process is called xylem transport. The water moves from the roots, through the stem, and finally reaches the leaves at the top of the plant.

The Phloem: The phloem tubes transport food (sugars) made during photosynthesis from the leaves to other parts of the plant that need it — such as the roots, fruits, and growing tips. This transport of food is called translocation.

But what causes the water to move upwards against gravity? There are two main forces:

  • Root pressure: The roots push water upwards into the stem.
  • Transpiration pull: As water evaporates from the leaves, it creates a "pull" that draws more water up from the roots (we will learn more about this later).
Key Concept: Xylem tubes in the stem transport water and minerals from roots to leaves.
Think About It: If you place a celery stalk in coloured water, the xylem tubes will transport the coloured water upwards. The colour will spread throughout the veins in the leaves. This is a visible way to see the transport system at work!

What Happens in the Leaves?

When water reaches the leaves, several important things happen:

1. Photosynthesis: Leaves use water (along with carbon dioxide and sunlight) to make food for the plant through photosynthesis. This is the plant's most important job—making its own food energy.

2. Cooling: Some of the water is used to keep the leaf cells turgid (firm and full of water), which helps the leaf stay strong and rigid.

3. Transpiration: Not all the water that reaches the leaves is used for photosynthesis or staying turgid. Some of it turns into water vapour and escapes into the air. This process is called transpiration.

Leaves have special structures called stomata (plural of stoma). These are tiny pores or openings on the leaf surface, mostly on the underside. Water vapour escapes through the stomata into the atmosphere. In fact, a large plant can lose many litres of water per day through transpiration!

Key Concept: Transpiration is the loss of water vapour from the leaves through stomata.

Understanding Transpiration

What is transpiration? Transpiration is the process by which plants lose water in the form of water vapour through the stomata in their leaves. It happens because water inside the leaf cells evaporates, especially when the leaf is exposed to sunlight and heat.

💧 Transpiration Process

Water is absorbed by roots, transported up through the stem, and escapes as water vapour through tiny pores (stomata) in the leaves. This continuous process is called transpiration!

Why does transpiration happen? Transpiration is not a "waste" process—it is actually very important for the plant. Here's why:

  • Creates a pull force: As water evaporates from the leaves, it creates a "pull" that draws water up from the roots (transpiration pull).
  • Cools the plant: Just like sweating cools your body, transpiration cools the leaves, especially on hot days.
  • Transports minerals: Water carries dissolved minerals up to the leaves. Transpiration ensures these minerals are delivered effectively.
  • Maintains cell turgidity: The water pressure inside cells keeps leaves and stems firm and prevents wilting.

How do stomata work? Stomata are surrounded by two special cells called guard cells. When the plant has plenty of water and it is light outside, the guard cells absorb water and swell up, opening the stoma. This allows carbon dioxide to enter the leaf for photosynthesis, and allows water vapour to escape.

When it is dark or when water is scarce, the guard cells lose water and shrink. This closes the stoma, reducing water loss. The plant is able to control the opening and closing of stomata to balance the need for photosynthesis with the need to conserve water.

Key Concept: Guard cells control the opening and closing of stomata to regulate transpiration.

Factors that affect transpiration:

  • Sunlight: More sunlight → higher temperature → more transpiration. Sunlight also opens the stomata wider.
  • Temperature: Higher temperatures increase transpiration. Water evaporates faster when it is hot.
  • Wind: Wind blows away the layer of water vapour around the leaf, allowing more water to evaporate.
  • Humidity: Lower humidity (drier air) increases transpiration. Higher humidity slows down transpiration.
  • Availability of water: If the soil is very dry, the plant closes its stomata to conserve water, reducing transpiration.

The Transpiration Pull: A Chain of Water

Now let's put everything together. Remember, water travels from the roots all the way to the leaves. But what keeps it moving upwards?

Imagine a chain of water molecules in the xylem, connected to each other. At the top of the chain, in the leaves, water molecules are constantly evaporating and escaping as water vapour through the stomata. As each water molecule leaves, it pulls the water molecule below it upwards. This continues down the entire chain, all the way back to the roots.

This is called the transpiration pull. It is one of the most powerful forces in nature! The transpiration pull can lift water and minerals hundreds of metres high, which is why tall trees can survive.

Here is the complete cycle:

  1. Roots absorb water and minerals from the soil through root hairs.
  2. Water and minerals travel up through xylem tubes in the stem.
  3. Water reaches the leaves and is used for photosynthesis.
  4. Excess water evaporates from the leaves through stomata (transpiration).
  5. As water evaporates, it pulls more water up from the roots (transpiration pull).
  6. The cycle continues as long as the plant is alive and has water in the soil.
Key Concept: Transpiration pull is the force created by water evaporating from leaves, which pulls water up from the roots.
Did You Know? In a rainforest, trees release so much water through transpiration that it forms a kind of "recycled rain." The water that was absorbed by tree roots eventually comes back down as rain, which is then absorbed by the roots again!

Why Is the Plant Transport System Important?

The plant transport system (roots, stem with xylem tubes, and leaves) is absolutely essential for plants to survive. Here is why:

1. Delivers water and minerals: The transport system carries water and minerals from the soil to all parts of the plant. These are essential nutrients needed for growth, development, and health.

2. Supports photosynthesis: Without water delivered to the leaves, photosynthesis cannot happen. Photosynthesis is the plant's main way of making food energy, so without it, the plant cannot survive or grow.

3. Maintains cell turgidity: The water transported through the stem keeps leaf and stem cells firm and turgid. This is why plants wilt when they do not get enough water—the cells lose water and become limp.

4. Cools the plant: Through transpiration, plants release water vapour that helps cool their leaves, especially in hot conditions. This prevents the leaves from overheating and being damaged.

5. Transports dissolved minerals: Water does not just move alone—it carries dissolved minerals that the plant needs for healthy growth. These minerals include nitrogen for leaf growth, phosphorus for root development, and potassium for overall plant health.

In short, without a working transport system, no plant could survive. It is one of the most important systems in the entire plant!

Section B: Open-Ended Questions (2-3 marks each)

6. A student placed a celery stalk in red-coloured water. After a few hours, she cut the stalk horizontally and observed red dots throughout the cut surface. Explain what the red dots show about how plants transport water.
Model Answer:

The red dots are the xylem tubes that have transported the coloured water upwards through the stem. They show that plants have tiny tubes inside their stems that carry water and minerals from the roots to the leaves. The red dots prove that water travels through these specific tubes (xylem) rather than spreading evenly throughout the stem.

Marking points:
  • Red dots are xylem tubes (1 mark)
  • They show water travels/is transported upwards (1 mark)
  • Explanation connects observation to plant transport system (1 mark)
7. A potted plant was placed in a sealed plastic bag. After 3 days, water droplets were observed on the inside of the plastic bag. Explain where this water came from and what process caused it.
Model Answer:

The water droplets came from the plant through the process of transpiration. Water absorbed by the plant's roots is transported up the stem to the leaves. In the leaves, excess water evaporates and escapes as water vapour through the stomata. Because the plastic bag is sealed, the water vapour cannot escape into the air and instead condenses on the inside of the bag, forming water droplets.

Marking points:
  • Water came from the plant (1 mark)
  • Process is transpiration (1 mark)
  • Explanation of how water vapour condenses in sealed bag (1 mark)
8. Explain why plants wilt (droop and become limp) when they do not receive enough water from the soil.
Model Answer:

When there is not enough water in the soil, the plant's roots cannot absorb enough water. With less water in the plant, the cells in the leaves and stems lose their turgidity (firmness). Turgidity is the water pressure inside cells that keeps them firm and rigid. Without enough water, the cells become flaccid (soft and limp), causing the leaves and stems to droop and wilt. The plant remains wilted until more water is absorbed by the roots.

Marking points:
  • Roots cannot absorb enough water (1 mark)
  • Cells lose water/turgidity (1 mark)
  • Cells become flaccid/limp, causing wilting (1 mark)

🎮 Try It Yourself - Transport Path Tracer

Follow the water's journey through the plant. Click each step to trace the path:

Step 1: Water Absorption (ROOTS)
Root hairs absorb water and minerals from the soil. The water enters the root cells by osmosis. Dissolved minerals are absorbed through active transport (using energy).
Step 2: Movement in Xylem (STEM)
The water enters the xylem vessels in the root. Root pressure forces water up into the stem. The xylem is like a system of tubes that carries water all the way up the plant.
Step 3: Arrival at Leaves (LEAVES)
Water travels up through the stem and reaches the leaves. The leaf veins contain xylem vessels that distribute water to all the leaf cells.
Step 4: Water Use in Leaves (PHOTOSYNTHESIS)
Leaf cells use the water for photosynthesis (making food). Some water also keeps cells firm and helps them stay upright. Water is essential for the leaf to function!
Step 5: Escape as Vapour (TRANSPIRATION)
Some water inside the leaf cells evaporates and turns into water vapour. This vapour escapes through tiny pores called stomata in the leaf surface. This is transpiration!
Step 6: The Cycle Continues
The water vapour rises into the atmosphere. Eventually it condenses into clouds and falls as rain, which returns to the soil. The plant roots absorb it again, and the cycle repeats!
