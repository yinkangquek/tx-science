Diversity of Living and Non-Living Things

Explore the fascinating world of living organisms and understand what makes them different from non-living objects

🎯 Learning Outcomes

2

📚 Interactive Notes

🌱 What Are Living Things?

Living things are organisms that have certain characteristics which non-living objects do not have. These characteristics help us identify and understand the diversity of life around us. Living things can be as small as a bacterium you cannot see with your naked eye, or as large as a blue whale!

Understanding Each Characteristic:

🔄 Movement - Can living things move? ▼

What it means: Living things can change position or move from one place to another.

Examples:

  • Animals like dogs, birds, and fish can walk, fly, or swim
  • Plants move too! Their leaves follow the sun (heliotropism), and roots grow downward
  • Single-celled bacteria move towards food or light

Did you know? Not all movement is obvious. Seeds can move by wind or water. Spores float through the air. These are still forms of movement!

💨 Respiration - How do living things breathe? ▼

What it means: Living things release energy from food using oxygen (in most cases). This is called respiration.

How it works: Living things take in oxygen and use it to break down food molecules. This releases energy that the organism needs to grow, move, and stay alive.

Examples:

  • Animals breathe in oxygen through lungs or gills
  • Plants take in oxygen through tiny holes called stomata on their leaves
  • Bacteria also respire, even though they are microscopic

Fun fact: When you breathe, you can feel your chest moving. That's respiration happening!

👁️ Sensitivity - Do living things respond to their surroundings? ▼

What it means: Living things detect changes in their environment and respond to them.

Examples:

  • You feel heat from the sun and move to shade
  • A flower bends towards sunlight
  • A pupil in your eye becomes smaller in bright light and larger in dim light
  • A plant's roots grow towards water in the soil
  • A dog hears a sound and runs towards it

Key point: Sensitivity helps living things survive in their environment. If something is dangerous, they respond quickly!

📏 Growth - How do living things grow? ▼

What it means: Living things increase in size and mass over time.

Important distinction: Growth is NOT the same as just getting bigger. Growth means the organism is adding new cells and getting heavier (more mass), not just stretching.

Examples:

  • A baby grows into a child and then into an adult
  • A seed grows into a seedling, then into a full plant
  • An egg hatches into a chick that grows into a chicken

Did you know? Some living things grow their whole lives (like some fish and reptiles), while others stop growing when they reach a certain age (like most mammals).

👶 Reproduction - How do living things make babies? ▼

What it means: Living things can produce offspring (babies) to continue their species.

Types of reproduction:

  • Sexual reproduction: Two parents combine to create offspring (like most animals)
  • Asexual reproduction: One parent creates offspring (like bacteria dividing, or plants growing from cuttings)

Examples:

  • Mammals like dogs produce puppies
  • Birds lay eggs that hatch into chicks
  • Plants produce seeds or spores
  • Bacteria reproduce by splitting in two
💩 Excretion - What happens to waste in living things? ▼

What it means: Living things produce waste products from their activities and must get rid of them.

Types of waste:

  • Feces: Undigested food that passes out
  • Urine: Waste from the kidneys
  • Carbon dioxide: Waste gas from respiration, exhaled when breathing out
  • Sweat: Water and salt that cool the body

Why it matters: If waste builds up inside, it can poison the living thing. Excretion keeps organisms healthy!

🍎 Nutrition - How do living things get food? ▼

What it means: Living things take in food and nutrients to give them energy and materials for growth.

Different types of nutrition:

  • Autotrophs: Make their own food (like plants using sunlight - photosynthesis)
  • Heterotrophs: Eat other organisms for food (like animals, fungi, and many bacteria)

Examples:

  • Plants use sunlight to make sugar (food)
  • Animals eat plants or other animals
  • Fungi absorb nutrients from dead matter

Food gives us: Energy to move and think, and materials to build and repair our bodies!

🆚 Living vs Non-Living Things

It might seem easy to tell the difference between living and non-living things, but nature has some tricky examples! Let's learn how to identify them carefully using the characteristics of life (MRS GREN).

Quick Rule: If something has even one or two characteristics of MRS GREN consistently, it's likely non-living. But if it has all seven, it's definitely living!

Side-by-Side Comparison:

Characteristic Living Things Non-Living Things
Movement Can move on their own or parts move Move only if pushed or forced
Respiration Release energy from food Do not use oxygen or release energy
Sensitivity Respond to light, heat, touch, sound Do not respond to surroundings
Growth Increase in size and mass naturally Don't grow (though they may get worn down)
Reproduction Make offspring of the same kind Cannot reproduce
Excretion Remove waste products Do not produce waste
Nutrition Take in and use food for energy Do not eat or need food

Tricky Examples to Think About:

🔥 Is a fire living or non-living? ▼

Let's check: Fire MOVES, RESPONDS to air, GROWS, and REPRODUCES (by spreading). It even "eats" fuel and "breathes" oxygen!

The answer: FIRE IS NON-LIVING

Why? Although fire shows some characteristics of living things, it cannot do all seven things. Most importantly, fire does not have cells - the basic units of life. It's a chemical reaction, not a living organism.

🌰 Is a seed living or non-living? ▼

The answer: A SEED IS LIVING

Why? A seed is dormant (resting) but still alive. Inside the seed:

  • There are living cells that are resting
  • Respiration still happens, but very slowly
  • It can respond to water and warmth by germinating (sprouting)
  • When conditions are right, it will grow and reproduce

Key learning: Just because something isn't moving or growing RIGHT NOW doesn't mean it's non-living!

🪨 Is a rock living or non-living? ▼

The answer: A ROCK IS NON-LIVING

Why? A rock has NO characteristics of MRS GREN:

  • It doesn't move on its own
  • It doesn't respire or need oxygen
  • It doesn't respond to its environment
  • It doesn't grow (though it might be broken down over millions of years)
  • It doesn't reproduce
  • It doesn't excrete waste
  • It doesn't need food or nutrition

However: Rocks can have living things ON them or IN them! Moss grows on rocks, bacteria live in cracks. The rock itself is non-living, but it can be a home for living things.

💭 Think About It:

Some viruses are hard to classify! They are not cells and cannot do all of MRS GREN on their own. But once inside a host cell, they can replicate. Scientists debate whether viruses are truly living or non-living!

🏷️ Classifying Living Things

With millions of different living things on Earth, scientists organize them into groups based on what they have in common. This is called classification. In Primary 3, we focus on the four major groups you see around you every day.

The Four Major Groups:

🌿 Plants | 🐾 Animals | 🍄 Fungi | 🦠 Bacteria

🌿 PLANTS

What is a plant?

Plants are living things that make their own food using sunlight, water, and air (a process called photosynthesis). They have roots to absorb water, stems to support them, and leaves to catch sunlight.

Two Main Types of Plants:
🌸 Flowering Plants (Angiosperms) ▼

Characteristics:

  • Have colorful flowers
  • Produce fruits and seeds inside fruits
  • Seeds are protected inside a seed coat
  • Most common type of plant on Earth
  • Include vegetables, fruits, trees with flowers

Examples: Roses, sunflowers, apple trees, tomato plants, orchids, grasses

How they reproduce: Flowers attract insects or use wind to spread pollen, creating seeds inside fruits

🌲 Non-Flowering Plants ▼

Characteristics:

  • Do NOT have flowers or fruits
  • Reproduce using spores instead of seeds
  • Spores are very tiny and spread by wind or water
  • Less common than flowering plants

Examples: Ferns, mosses, club mosses

How they reproduce: Release spores that grow into new plants

Interesting fact: Ferns are much older than flowers! They were here long before dinosaurs!

🐾 ANIMALS

What is an animal?

Animals are living things that cannot make their own food. They must eat plants or other animals to survive. All animals have the ability to move from place to place and have sense organs to detect their surroundings.

Six Main Groups of Animals:

Group Key Characteristics Examples Body Covering Reproduction
🐴 Mammals Warm-blooded, have hair/fur, feed milk to babies Dogs, cats, humans, whales, bats Hair or fur Live birth
🦅 Birds Warm-blooded, have wings, lay eggs Chickens, eagles, sparrows, penguins Feathers Lay eggs
🐠 Fish Cold-blooded, live in water, have gills Goldfish, sharks, salmon, tuna Scales Lay eggs
🦎 Reptiles Cold-blooded, lay eggs, dry skin Snakes, lizards, crocodiles, turtles Dry scales Lay eggs
🐸 Amphibians Cold-blooded, live in water and on land, moist skin Frogs, toads, newts, salamanders Moist, smooth skin Lay eggs in water
🐛 Insects Small, 6 legs, 3 body parts, exoskeleton Ants, butterflies, bees, beetles Hard shell (exoskeleton) Lay eggs

🐴 Mammals

  • Warm-blooded
  • Have hair or fur on skin
  • Feed milk to babies
  • Most have 4 legs (some have 2 or 0)
  • Most give birth to live babies
  • Have a backbone
Examples: Humans, dogs, cats, lions, elephants, whales, bats

🦅 Birds

  • Warm-blooded
  • Have feathers
  • Have 2 legs and 2 wings
  • Lay eggs with hard shells
  • Beaks instead of teeth
  • Lay eggs
Examples: Eagles, sparrows, pigeons, parrots, penguins, chickens

🐠 Fish

  • Cold-blooded
  • Have scales (smooth)
  • Breathe through gills
  • Have fins for swimming
  • Lay eggs in water
  • Live in water
Examples: Goldfish, sharks, salmon, cod, tuna, seahorses

🦎 Reptiles

  • Cold-blooded
  • Have dry, scaly skin
  • Lay eggs (most)
  • Crawl or slither
  • Breathe air through lungs
  • Have backbone
Examples: Snakes, lizards, crocodiles, turtles, iguanas, chameleons

🐸 Amphibians

  • Cold-blooded
  • Moist, smooth skin
  • Live part of life in water, part on land
  • Lay eggs in water
  • Breathe through skin and lungs
  • Examples: frogs live as tadpoles in water
Examples: Frogs, toads, salamanders, newts, caecilians

🐛 Insects

  • Have 6 legs (not 4 or 8)
  • Body divided into 3 parts
  • Hard outer shell (exoskeleton)
  • Most have wings
  • Lay eggs
  • Can go through metamorphosis
Examples: Butterflies, ants, bees, beetles, grasshoppers, dragonflies
Warm-Blooded vs Cold-Blooded:
  • Warm-blooded: Keep body temperature constant (Mammals, Birds) - even when outside is cold
  • Cold-blooded: Body temperature changes with environment (Fish, Reptiles, Amphibians) - warm when sun is hot, cold when it's cool

🍄 FUNGI

What are fungi?

Fungi are living things that are NOT plants, NOT animals, and NOT bacteria. They are a completely different kingdom! They cannot make their own food like plants do. Instead, they absorb nutrients from dead or rotting matter.

Key Differences from Plants ▼
Feature Plants Fungi
Food Making Make own food using sunlight (photosynthesis) Cannot make food, absorb from dead matter
Cell Wall Made of cellulose Made of chitin (like insect shells)
Structure Roots, stems, leaves Hyphae (thread-like filaments)
Chlorophyll Have green pigment (chlorophyll) No chlorophyll - not green
Reproduction Seeds or spores Spores only
Three Common Fungi You Know:

🍄 Mushrooms

  • Visible fruiting body (the part you see)
  • Umbrella-shaped with gills underneath
  • Release spores from gills
  • Most grow on the ground or wood
Role: Decomposers - break down dead wood and leaves, returning nutrients to soil

🔬 Mould (Mold)

  • Fuzzy growth on food or damp places
  • Usually green, blue, or black
  • Grows on bread, cheese, fruit, walls
  • Cannot see individual hyphae without microscope
Role: Decomposer - breaks down dead organic matter; some moulds are useful (like in cheese)

🧪 Yeast

  • Single-celled fungus (microscopic)
  • Used in bread-making and brewing
  • Produces carbon dioxide gas (makes bread fluffy)
  • Ferments sugars
Role: Helpful fungus - makes bread rise, ferments beverages

🦠 BACTERIA

What are bacteria?

Bacteria are the SMALLEST living things on Earth. They are so tiny you cannot see them without a powerful microscope. A single bacterium is just one cell, but it can do all seven things of MRS GREN!

📊 Characteristics of Bacteria ▼
  • Single-celled: Each bacterium is just ONE cell
  • Microscopic: Too small to see without a microscope - about 0.0005 mm
  • No nucleus: Their DNA floats freely, not in a nucleus like plant/animal cells
  • Very fast reproduction: Can divide every 20 minutes under good conditions
  • Everywhere: In soil, water, air, food, and even on your skin!
  • Ancient: Bacteria were the first living things on Earth, 3.8 billion years ago
Good Bacteria vs Harmful Bacteria ▼
✅ HELPFUL BACTERIA (Good Bacteria)
  • In yogurt: Help digest milk and produce yogurt
  • In our gut: Help us digest food properly
  • In soil: Break down dead matter and make nutrients available for plants
  • In sewage treatment: Clean wastewater
  • In medicine: Used to make antibiotics and vaccines
  • In compost: Help break down organic waste
❌ HARMFUL BACTERIA (Pathogenic Bacteria)
  • Cause diseases: Strep throat, food poisoning, skin infections
  • Spoil food: Make food go bad and create toxins
  • Cause infections: Can infect wounds and cause serious illness
  • Examples: E. coli, Salmonella, Staphylococcus
💡 Key Learning:

Not all bacteria are bad! In fact, most bacteria are helpful. Your body contains trillions of helpful bacteria! Staying clean and following food safety helps us avoid harmful bacteria.

✏️ PSLE-Style Practice Questions

Section A: Multiple Choice Questions

Choose the best answer for each question. These questions test your understanding, not just memorization.
Question 1 of 5
Tom observed that a wooden log does not move on its own and does not eat food. He concluded that the wooden log is non-living. Which of the following best explains why Tom's conclusion may not be correct based on his observation alone?
Model Answer: B
Tom only observed two characteristics (no movement, no eating), but living things must show ALL characteristics of MRS GREN, not just some. We need to observe more carefully - the log could contain living organisms inside it (like bacteria or fungi), or the log itself might be in a dormant state.
Question 2 of 5
A scientist found a new organism in the deep ocean. It has a cell wall made of chitin, absorbs nutrients from dead organisms around it, and has no chlorophyll. Which group of living things does this organism most likely belong to?
Model Answer: C
The key clues are: (1) chitin cell wall - this is characteristic of fungi, not plants (cellulose) or animals (no cell wall), (2) absorbs nutrients from dead matter - this is how fungi feed, (3) no chlorophyll - fungi don't make their own food like plants do. Therefore, it's a fungus.
Question 3 of 5
Sarah planted a seed in soil and watered it regularly. The seed germinated and grew into a plant. Which of the following best explains what happened during germination and growth?
Model Answer: B
A seed is living but dormant (resting). When provided with water, warmth, and oxygen, the cells inside begin to respire and divide, causing the seed to grow. This is germination - the seed "wakes up" and develops into a seedling. The seed was never dead; it was just inactive.
Question 4 of 5
Which pair of characteristics can be used to distinguish between a fish and a mammal that lives in water (like a dolphin)?
Model Answer: A
The best distinguishing features are: (1) Respiration - fish use gills to extract oxygen from water; dolphins have lungs and must come to the surface to breathe air, (2) Reproduction - fish lay eggs; dolphins give birth to live young and feed them milk. These are fundamental differences that clearly separate these two groups.
Question 5 of 5
A student observed mould growing on bread and bacteria in yogurt. Although both are living things, what is one major difference in how they obtain nutrients?
Model Answer: B
Mould absorbs nutrients from dead or decomposing matter (like the bread). Bacteria are diverse and obtain nutrients in different ways depending on the species - some absorb from their environment, some are heterotrophs that consume other organisms, and some are autotrophs that make their own food. This diversity is one reason bacteria are so successful!

Section B: Open-Ended Questions

Write your answer in the space provided. Show your thinking clearly.
Question 6 of 8
Describe how a frog demonstrates all seven characteristics of living things (MRS GREN). Give one real example for each characteristic.
Model Answer
M - Movement: A frog hops from one lily pad to another to escape predators.
R - Respiration: A frog breathes air through its lungs and releases carbon dioxide.
S - Sensitivity: A frog's tongue darts out and catches insects when it senses movement.
G - Growth: A tadpole grows into a larger frog over several weeks.
R - Reproduction: Male frogs croak to attract females and lay eggs in ponds.
E - Excretion: A frog passes urine and faeces to remove waste.
N - Nutrition: A frog eats insects, which give it energy and materials for growth.
(Other valid examples can be accepted as long as they clearly demonstrate each characteristic.)
Question 7 of 8
Compare and contrast plants and fungi. How are they similar? How are they different? (Think about how they get food, their structure, and their role in the environment.)
Model Answer
Similarities: Both are living things that don't move from place to place. Both have cell walls. Both reproduce using spores (fungi use spores; many plants also use spores). Both are found in similar environments like soil and forests.

Differences: Plants make their own food using sunlight and chlorophyll; fungi cannot make food and must absorb nutrients from dead matter. Plant cell walls are made of cellulose; fungal cell walls are made of chitin. Plants have roots, stems, and leaves; fungi have hyphae (thread-like structures).

Role: Plants produce food and oxygen for other organisms. Fungi decompose dead matter and recycle nutrients back to soil, helping plants grow.
Question 8 of 8
Bacteria are found everywhere - in soil, water, air, and on our bodies. Explain why bacteria can be both helpful and harmful. Give one example of each.
Model Answer
Why bacteria can be both helpful and harmful: Bacteria have different ways of getting nutrients and living. Some bacteria help other organisms, while others can cause damage or disease.

Helpful example: Bacteria in yogurt break down milk and turn it into yogurt. These bacteria help our digestion when we eat yogurt.

Harmful example: Salmonella bacteria can contaminate food and cause food poisoning, making people sick with stomach problems.

Key point: Not all bacteria are bad! In fact, most bacteria are helpful. Staying clean and practicing good food safety helps us avoid harmful bacteria while benefiting from helpful ones.

💚 Values, Ethics & Attitudes

  • Respect for Living Things: All living things play important roles in nature. Even small organisms like bacteria and fungi are valuable. We should care for all living things and avoid harming them unnecessarily.
  • Curiosity & Observation: Scientists learn by carefully observing the natural world. Practice patient observation - look closely at the living things around you and ask questions!
  • Responsibility: We share the Earth with millions of species. We have a responsibility to protect habitats and keep our environment clean and healthy for all living things.
  • Hygiene & Health: Understanding that bacteria can be harmful helps us practice good hygiene. Washing hands, cleaning food, and staying clean protects us and others from disease.
  • Wonder & Awe: The diversity of life is amazing! From tiny bacteria to enormous whales, from beautiful flowers to strange deep-sea creatures - nature is full of wonders to explore and appreciate.
