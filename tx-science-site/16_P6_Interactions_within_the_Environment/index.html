Extensive Learning Notes

What Is an Ecosystem?

An ecosystem is a community of living organisms (animals, plants, and microorganisms) interacting with their physical environment (soil, water, air, light, and temperature). Every ecosystem is unique and contains countless relationships between organisms and their surroundings.

Think of an ecosystem as a complex web where everything is connected. A forest ecosystem is very different from a pond ecosystem, yet both follow similar principles of energy flow and nutrient cycling.

Key Concept: An ecosystem consists of biotic components (all living things) and abiotic components (non-living physical factors). Both work together to maintain balance.

Living Components (Biotic)

  • Plants: Provide food and oxygen. Examples: flowers, trees, grass, algae
  • Animals: Eat plants or other animals. Examples: insects, birds, fish, mammals
  • Microorganisms: Break down dead matter. Examples: bacteria, fungi

Non-Living Components (Abiotic)

  • Air: Contains oxygen, carbon dioxide, and nitrogen needed by organisms
  • Water: Essential for all life; transports nutrients
  • Light: Energy source for plants through photosynthesis
  • Temperature: Affects organism metabolism and activity
  • Soil: Provides nutrients and minerals for plant growth

Examples of Ecosystems

Pond Ecosystem: Water plants, fish, frogs, insects, algae. Abiotic: water, sunlight, temperature, dissolved oxygen, pond sediment.

Forest Ecosystem: Trees, shrubs, birds, mammals, insects, fungi, soil organisms. Abiotic: sunlight, rainfall, temperature, soil nutrients, decomposing leaves.

Garden Ecosystem: Flowers, vegetables, soil organisms, insects, birds. Abiotic: sunlight, water from rain/irrigation, garden soil, air.

Did You Know? Microorganisms are invisible to the naked eye, but they are the most important decomposers in any ecosystem. A single gram of soil can contain billions of bacteria!

Organisms and Their Roles in an Ecosystem

Every organism in an ecosystem has a specific role or niche. Understanding these roles helps us see how energy flows through nature and how organisms depend on each other.

Producers

Producers are plants and other organisms that make their own food using energy from the sun. They use a process called photosynthesis to convert sunlight into chemical energy stored in food (glucose). All ecosystems start with producers because they capture the sun's energy.

  • Green plants: Use sunlight, water, and carbon dioxide to make glucose
  • Algae: Single-celled or simple aquatic plants that also photosynthesise
  • Cyanobacteria: Bacteria that can photosynthesise

Equation for photosynthesis: Carbon dioxide + Water + Light energy → Glucose + Oxygen

Consumers

Consumers are organisms that eat other organisms to get energy. They cannot make their own food and must eat producers or other consumers. Consumers are classified by what they eat:

  • Primary Consumers (Herbivores): Eat only plants. They convert plant energy into animal tissue.
    Examples: Rabbits, deer, caterpillars, grasshoppers, cows
  • Secondary Consumers (Carnivores/Omnivores): Eat primary consumers. Some may also eat plants.
    Examples: Frogs (eat insects), snakes (eat frogs), cats (eat mice), bears (eat fish and berries)
  • Tertiary Consumers (Top Carnivores): Eat secondary consumers and are rarely eaten by other animals.
    Examples: Eagles (eat snakes and frogs), lions (eat antelopes), sharks (eat fish)
Key Concept: Energy transfers from one organism to another, but only about 10% of energy is passed to the next level. The rest is used for growth, movement, and heat. This is why there are fewer predators than prey!

Decomposers

Decomposers break down dead organic matter and waste products, releasing nutrients back into the soil. Without decomposers, dead organisms would pile up and nutrients would be locked away. Decomposers complete the cycle of life.

  • Bacteria: Microscopic decomposers found in soil and water
  • Fungi (mushrooms, moulds): Feed on dead wood, leaves, and organic matter
  • Earthworms: Burrow through soil, breaking down organic material and aerating soil
  • Millipedes and beetles: Help break down dead leaves and plant material

Food Chains: Energy Flow in Ecosystems

A food chain is a sequence showing how energy passes from one organism to another. It starts with a producer and shows the flow of energy through consumers to decomposers. The arrows in a food chain point in the direction of energy flow, not the direction of eating!

🔬 Simple Food Chain

How to Read and Draw a Food Chain

In a food chain, the arrow (→) means "is eaten by" or "provides energy to". The energy flows FROM left TO right. Each organism depends on the organism(s) to its left for energy.

Understanding Energy Loss

When a plant captures 10,000 units of sun energy, only about 1,000 units become plant tissue. When an insect eats the plant, it only retains about 100 units (10% of what it ate). When a bird eats the insect, it keeps only 10 units. This is why:

  • There are many more plants than herbivores
  • There are many more herbivores than carnivores
  • The food chain is usually only 3-5 levels long
Think About It: Why would a carnivore that only eats meat need more territory (space) than a vegetarian herbivore eating the same type of plant? Think about the energy transfer!

Food Webs: Multiple Interconnected Chains

In reality, ecosystems are more complex than simple food chains. Food webs show multiple food chains interconnected, because most organisms eat more than one type of food, and are eaten by multiple predators. A food web gives a much more realistic picture of feeding relationships in an ecosystem.

🔬 Complex Food Web

Why Food Webs Matter

  • Most organisms have multiple food sources: A bird may eat seeds, insects, and berries
  • Multiple predators: A mouse is eaten by snakes, owls, and foxes
  • Shows ecosystem stability: If one food source disappears, an organism can switch to another
  • Reveals energy pathways: Shows all possible routes energy can take

Example Pond Food Web

Producers: Plants (algae, water weeds) → Capture sun's energy

Primary Consumers: Water insects, small fish, tadpoles → Eat plants

Secondary Consumers: Frogs, larger fish → Eat insects and smaller fish

Tertiary Consumers: Herons, eagles → Eat frogs and fish

Plants eaten by insects, tadpoles, small fish; eaten by frogs, larger fish; eaten by herons, eagles

All dead matter goes to decomposers (bacteria, fungi) which return nutrients to soil

Decomposers in Every Web

Don't forget that decomposers are part of every food web! Dead organisms, fallen leaves, and animal waste feed decomposers. They break down this material and return nutrients to the soil, where plants can use them. Without decomposers, nutrient cycling would stop.

Population Changes: Ripple Effects in Ecosystems

Ecosystems are balanced systems. When the population of one organism changes, it creates ripple effects throughout the entire food web. Understanding these relationships helps us predict what happens to an ecosystem when conditions change.

If a Producer Increases (More Plants)

Effect: Primary consumers have more food available.
Result:

  • Herbivore populations increase (they have plenty to eat)
  • More herbivores available for predators to eat
  • Predator populations may increase

If a Producer Decreases (Fewer Plants - e.g., drought)

Effect: Primary consumers have less food.
Result:

  • Herbivore populations decrease (starving)
  • Fewer prey available for carnivores
  • Carnivore populations decrease (less food)
  • This continues up the food chain

If a Primary Consumer Increases (More Herbivores)

Effect: More predators get fed, and more plants are eaten.
Result:

  • Predator populations increase (abundant food)
  • Plant populations decrease (more being eaten)

If a Predator Increases (More Carnivores)

Effect: More prey are eaten.
Result:

  • Prey populations decrease
  • Predator populations may eventually decrease (not enough food)
  • This creates a predator-prey cycle
Key Concept: Ecosystems seek equilibrium or balance. When one population increases, it checks another, preventing any single organism from taking over completely.

How Organisms Are Adapted to Their Environment

Organisms have special features and behaviours (called adaptations) that help them survive in their specific ecosystem. Adaptations allow organisms to find food, avoid predators, survive climate, and reproduce.

Physical Adaptations (Body Features)

  • Plant adaptations: Waxy leaves to reduce water loss, deep roots to find water, thorns to protect from herbivores
  • Animal predator adaptations: Sharp claws and teeth for hunting, keen eyesight, silent movement
  • Animal prey adaptations: Camouflage to hide, fast running speed, spines or shells for protection
  • Climate adaptations: Thick fur in cold areas, light colours in hot deserts, webbed feet for water animals

Behavioural Adaptations (Actions)

  • Migration: Animals move to new areas seasonally for food or breeding
  • Hibernation: Animals enter deep sleep during harsh winters
  • Nocturnal behaviour: Animals are active at night to avoid daytime heat or predators
  • Hunting strategies: Lions hunt in packs, spiders build webs to trap prey

Human Activities and Environmental Impact

Humans have enormous power to change ecosystems. Many human activities disrupt the balance of nature and harm both organisms and the ecosystem services we depend on. Understanding these impacts is crucial for conservation and sustainable living.

Major Human Impacts on the Environment

1. Deforestation and Habitat Loss

What happens: Trees are cut down for timber, farming, or development.
Impact on ecosystem:

  • Habitat destroyed → organisms lose homes
  • Producer (trees) removed → food chain breaks
  • Animal populations decrease or go extinct
  • Soil erosion occurs (trees hold soil in place)
  • Carbon dioxide increases (trees absorb CO2)
2. Pollution

Air pollution: Gases from vehicles and factories harm breathing, reduce photosynthesis
Water pollution: Chemicals in rivers kill fish and aquatic plants
Soil pollution: Pesticides and heavy metals poison organisms and crop plants

3. Overfishing

What happens: More fish are caught than can be naturally replaced.
Impact: Fish populations collapse → seabirds and marine mammals starve → entire ocean ecosystems collapse

4. Climate Change

Rising temperatures: Plants bloom at wrong times, animals can't find food
Changing rainfall: Droughts kill plants, floods destroy habitats
Rising sea levels: Coastal habitats disappear

5. Introduction of Invasive Species

What happens: Non-native organisms are introduced (accidentally or on purpose).
Impact: Invasive species compete with native species, eat them, or introduce diseases. Native organisms go extinct.

Conservation and Solutions

We can reduce our impact and protect ecosystems:

  • Protected areas: National parks and nature reserves preserve habitats
  • Sustainable fishing: Catch limits and protected breeding areas
  • Reforestation: Plant new trees to restore forests
  • Renewable energy: Solar and wind instead of fossil fuels
  • Reduce, reuse, recycle: Less waste to landfills and oceans
  • Education: Learning about ecosystems helps people make better choices
Did You Know? Bees are responsible for pollinating about 75% of global food crops. If bee populations collapse due to habitat loss and pesticides, we lose a critical link in our food webs!

Question 1
Which of the following is an abiotic component of a forest ecosystem?
(A) Trees and shrubs
(B) Birds and insects
(C) Rainfall and temperature
(D) Fungi and bacteria
Question 2
In a food chain: Grass → Rabbit → Fox, the rabbit is a _____.
(A) Producer
(B) Primary consumer
(C) Secondary consumer
(D) Decomposer
Question 3
What is the role of decomposers in an ecosystem?
(A) To hunt and eat primary consumers
(B) To produce food using sunlight
(C) To break down dead matter and return nutrients to soil
(D) To control the population of herbivores
Question 4
If the population of insects in a food web suddenly decreases, which organisms would be directly affected?
(A) Only the plants
(B) Only the insects that eat plants
(C) Organisms that eat insects and the insects themselves
(D) Only the top predators like eagles
Question 5
Which human activity is most likely to destroy habitat and cause extinction of organisms?
(A) Using renewable energy sources
(B) Deforestation for agriculture and development
(C) Protecting wildlife in national parks
(D) Planting trees in empty areas
Section B: Open-Ended Questions (3-4 marks each)
Question 6
A pond ecosystem is affected by a sudden drop in temperature due to an unusually cold winter. Using your knowledge of food chains and ecosystems, explain:
(a) Why would the population of water plants be affected? (1 mark)
(b) How would this affect the water insects that eat the plants? (1 mark)
(c) What would happen to the fish population that depends on insects for food? (1 mark)
Question 7
Describe the role of decomposers in maintaining the balance of an ecosystem. In your answer, explain what would happen if decomposers suddenly disappeared. (4 marks)
Question 8
In a pond ecosystem, the number of frogs suddenly decreased due to a disease. Predict and explain what would happen to:
(a) The population of insects in the pond (2 marks)
(b) The population of snakes that hunt frogs (2 marks)

Environmental Stewardship

Understanding ecosystems teaches us that all living things are connected. Every organism—from the tiniest bacteria to the mightiest animal—plays a role. Our actions as humans have far-reaching consequences. By learning about food chains, ecosystems, and how environments work, we become empowered to make choices that protect nature for future generations. Conservation is everyone's responsibility.
