Interactive Notes

What Are Materials?

Everything around us is made of materials. A material is a substance that things are made of. Materials can be:

  • Solid - has a fixed shape (wood, plastic, metal)
  • Liquid - takes the shape of its container (water, oil)
  • Gas - spreads to fill the space available (air, oxygen)
Key Concept: Most objects around us are made of one or more materials. For example, a pencil is made of wood, graphite (lead), and metal.

Different materials have different properties. Let's think about cups:

  • A ceramic cup is hard and breaks easily, but keeps drinks hot
  • A glass cup is transparent so we can see inside, but also breaks easily
  • A plastic cup is flexible, lightweight, and doesn't break easily
  • A metal cup is very strong and conducts heat quickly
Did You Know? The same object can be made from many different materials! Engineers and designers choose materials based on what properties are needed for that specific object.
Think About It: Look around your classroom right now. Can you identify at least 5 different materials? What are they used to make?

Properties of Materials

A property is a characteristic or feature of a material. Understanding material properties helps us choose the right material for the right job.

🔍 Material Properties at a Glance
Flexibility: Can It Bend? ▼

Flexibility means a material can bend or flex without breaking.

Flexible Materials Non-Flexible Materials
Rubber band (stretches easily) Glass (breaks when bent)
Paper (folds without breaking) Ceramic (cracks when bent)
Fabric (bends with clothing) Hard plastic (can crack)
Metal wire (can be bent into shapes) Chalk (breaks easily)
Example: Rubber bands are used for bundling things because they're flexible. They stretch and then return to their original shape without breaking.
Waterproofness: Does It Keep Water Out? ▼

Waterproofness means a material does not allow water to pass through it.

Waterproof Materials Non-Waterproof Materials
Plastic (repels water) Paper (absorbs water)
Rubber (used for raincoats) Cotton (fabric absorbs water)
Metal (water doesn't soak through) Leather (absorbs water)
Glass (water sits on surface) Wood (absorbs and swells)
Example: Umbrellas are made of waterproof materials like nylon or polyester because they need to keep rain from getting through.
Strength: How Strong Is It? ▼

Strength means a material can support heavy loads or resist breaking under pressure.

Strong Materials Weak Materials
Steel (used for buildings, cars) Tissue paper (tears easily)
Concrete (supports heavy weight) Foam (crushes under weight)
Aluminum (strong but light) Cardboard (can be crushed)
Titanium (used in aircraft) Thin plastic (tears or bends)
Example: Bridges are built from steel and concrete because these materials are very strong and can support the weight of cars and people crossing the bridge.
Transparency: Can You See Through It? ▼

Transparency describes how much light passes through a material:

  • Transparent - You can see clearly through it (clear glass, clear plastic)
  • Translucent - Light passes through but you can't see clearly (frosted glass, tracing paper)
  • Opaque - No light passes through (metal, wood, most plastics)
Transparent Translucent Opaque
Clear glass Frosted glass Wood
Clear plastic sheet Tracing paper Metal
Pure water Wax paper Concrete
Diamond Thin cloth Plastic bag (colored)
Example: Windows in houses are made of transparent glass because we want to see outside while keeping the wind and rain out.
Float or Sink: Does It Float in Water? ▼

Buoyancy is the ability of a material to float or sink in water. This depends on the density of the material.

Materials That Float Materials That Sink
Wood (less dense than water) Metal (denser than water)
Cork (very light) Glass (denser than water)
Foam (air pockets make it light) Stone (very dense)
Some plastics (depends on density) Ceramic (hard and dense)
Example: Boats are made of steel (which normally sinks), but they float because of their shape, which contains air pockets that reduce the overall density.
Magnetic: Is It Attracted to Magnets? ▼

Magnetism is the property that describes whether a material is attracted to magnets.

Magnetic Materials Non-Magnetic Materials
Iron (attracts magnets strongly) Plastic (no attraction)
Steel (contains iron) Wood (no attraction)
Nickel (attracts magnets) Glass (no attraction)
Cobalt (attracts magnets) Rubber (no attraction)
Example: Paper clips are made of steel because they need to be picked up by magnets. If they were made of plastic, magnets couldn't pick them up.

Natural vs Man-Made Materials

Materials come from two sources: nature and factories. Understanding the difference helps us appreciate the resources we use.

Natural Materials Man-Made Materials
Source: Come from nature directly Source: Made by people in factories
Examples: Cotton, wool, wood, leather, rubber, silk, stone Examples: Plastic, glass, metal alloys, nylon, polyester, concrete
Processing: Minimal processing needed Processing: Complex manufacturing required
Biodegradable: Most decompose naturally Biodegradable: Many take hundreds of years to decompose
Natural Materials Examples:
  • Cotton - comes from cotton plants, used for clothes and sheets
  • Wood - from trees, used for furniture, houses, and paper
  • Rubber - from rubber trees, used for erasers, tires, and shoes
  • Wool - from sheep, used for sweaters and blankets
  • Leather - from animal hides, used for shoes and belts
  • Silk - from silkworm cocoons, used for luxury fabrics
Man-Made Materials Examples:
  • Plastic - made from crude oil, used for bags, bottles, and toys
  • Glass - made from melted sand, used for windows and bottles
  • Nylon - synthetic fiber, used for stockings and ropes
  • Polyester - synthetic fiber, used for clothing
  • Concrete - made from cement and stone, used for buildings
  • Aluminum - extracted from bauxite ore, used for cans and aircraft
Did You Know? Scientists are creating new "bio-based" materials made from plants! For example, some plastics are now made from corn starch instead of crude oil. These are more sustainable for our planet.
Think About It: Natural materials are renewable (we can grow more cotton and trees), but man-made materials like plastic are made from non-renewable resources. How can we use fewer plastic products in our daily lives?

Choosing the Right Material

Engineers, designers, and manufacturers must choose the best material for each object based on what properties are needed. Let's look at some real examples:

Example 1: Raincoat Material ▼

What properties does a raincoat need?

  • ✓ Waterproof - must keep water out
  • ✓ Flexible - must bend when we move
  • ✓ Durable - must last a long time
  • ✓ Light - shouldn't be too heavy

Best material: Rubber or synthetic waterproof fabric (nylon, polyester)

❌ Not suitable: Cotton (absorbs water), glass (can't bend), paper (tears easily)

Example 2: Window Material ▼

What properties does a window need?

  • ✓ Transparent - must let light through
  • ✓ Strong - must not break easily
  • ✓ Rigid - must keep its shape
  • ✓ Waterproof - must keep rain out

Best material: Glass or clear plastic

❌ Not suitable: Wood (opaque), paper (tears), rubber (not transparent)

Example 3: Bridge Material ▼

What properties does a bridge need?

  • ✓ Very Strong - must support heavy cars and trucks
  • ✓ Rigid - must not bend or flex
  • ✓ Durable - must last for many years
  • ✓ Weather-resistant - must withstand rain and sun

Best materials: Steel and concrete

❌ Not suitable: Plastic (not strong enough), wood (rots over time), rubber (too flexible)

Example 4: Toy Ball Material ▼

What properties does a toy ball need?

  • ✓ Flexible/Bouncy - must bounce when thrown
  • ✓ Lightweight - easy to throw
  • ✓ Durable - must survive lots of use
  • ✓ Safe - must not be toxic

Best materials: Rubber or plastic

❌ Not suitable: Glass (breaks and dangerous), metal (too heavy), ceramic (breaks)

Key Concept: The best material for a job depends on:
  • What properties are needed
  • How long it needs to last
  • How much it costs
  • How much it weighs
  • Environmental impact

Assessment: PSLE-Style Questions

Section A: Multiple Choice Questions (Choose 1 answer)
1. Which of the following is a natural material?
2. Sarah wants to make an umbrella. Which material would be most suitable?
3. Which material property allows a rubber band to stretch and return to its original shape?
4. A glass window is made of glass instead of plastic because glass is needed for which property?
5. Which statement about materials is TRUE?
Section B: Open-Ended Questions (Write Your Answer)
6. Sarah wants to build a bridge for her toy cars. She has three materials to choose from: cardboard, wooden sticks, and steel. Which material should she choose and why? Explain using the properties of materials.
Model Answer: Sarah should choose steel. Steel is the most suitable material because:
  • It is very strong and can support heavy loads (the toy cars)
  • It is rigid and will not bend or break under weight
  • It is durable and will last a long time
  • Cardboard is weak and will collapse. Wooden sticks are stronger but still not strong enough for a bridge that needs to support repeated weight
7. Look at the materials: rubber, glass, and wood. For each material, give ONE example of an object it could be used for and explain WHY that material is suitable for that object (use material properties).
Model Answer:
  • Rubber: Used for shoe soles because rubber is flexible (bends when we walk) and has grip
  • Glass: Used for drinking glasses because glass is transparent (we can see the drink) and waterproof (it holds liquid without leaking)
  • Wood: Used for chairs and tables because wood is strong (can support weight), rigid (keeps its shape), and durable (lasts a long time)
8. Your family wants to reduce plastic use and protect the environment. Suggest how you could replace plastic bags with a natural material. Explain why this natural material would be suitable.
Model Answer: We could use cotton bags instead of plastic bags because:
  • Cotton is a natural material that comes from plants
  • It is strong enough to carry items without tearing
  • It is flexible and can be folded for storage
  • Cotton is biodegradable - it decomposes naturally and doesn't pollute the environment like plastic
  • Cotton bags can be washed and reused many times

Note: Other acceptable answers could include: linen bags, paper bags, or reusable plastic bags (though less ideal for the environment).

Values, Ethics & Attitudes: Responsible Material Use

Materials are valuable resources. Many come from our environment, and we have a responsibility to use them wisely.

  • Recycling: Materials like plastic, glass, aluminum, and paper can be recycled. Recycling means we can use these materials again instead of throwing them away.
  • Reusing: Before throwing away an object, think if it can be reused for something else. Old plastic bags can be storage. Old containers can hold things.
  • Reducing: Use less material. Choose products with minimal packaging. Use reusable bags and water bottles instead of single-use items.
  • Choosing Sustainable Materials: When possible, choose natural and biodegradable materials. Look for products made from recycled materials.
  • Caring for Our Planet: Non-biodegradable materials like plastic can damage ecosystems and harm wildlife. By being mindful of our material choices, we protect the environment for future generations.

Challenge Yourself: For one week, track all the materials you use. How many items could be replaced with more sustainable alternatives?
