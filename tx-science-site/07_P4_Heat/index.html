What Is Heat?

Understanding Heat

Heat is a form of energy that can be transferred from one object to another. It is the energy that makes things hot or cold. Heat always moves from places that are hotter to places that are cooler—like how the steam from hot tea rises into the cooler air around it.

Heat vs. Temperature

Many people confuse heat and temperature, but they are different:

  • Temperature measures how hot or cold something is. We measure it in degrees Celsius (°C) using a thermometer.
  • Heat is the energy that transfers between objects because of their temperature difference. It flows from the hotter object to the cooler one.
Temperature is just a measurement. Heat is energy moving from hot to cold. Think of it this way: a cup of hot water has a high temperature, but a swimming pool full of warm water contains much more heat energy, even if its temperature is lower. Heat is about energy, not just how "hot" something feels.

How We Get Heat

Heat can come from many sources:

  • The Sun – Our biggest source of heat energy on Earth
  • Fire and Burning – Burning wood, gas, or coal releases heat
  • Friction – Rubbing your hands together creates heat
  • Electricity – Light bulbs, electric heaters, and cookers use electricity to create heat
  • Chemical Reactions – Mixing certain chemicals releases heat
The fastest way to start a fire using friction is called a "bow drill." In ancient times, people would spin a wooden stick against another piece of wood using a bow, and friction would create enough heat to start a fire!

How Heat Flows

The Direction of Heat

Heat always flows in one direction: from the hotter object to the cooler object. This is so important that scientists call it a law of nature. Heat will never naturally flow from something cold to something hot on its own.

Everyday Examples of Heat Flow

  • Hot drink cooling down – When you pour hot tea into a cup, the heat energy flows out of the tea into the cooler air and cup. That's why the tea gets cooler over time.
  • Ice cream melting – Heat from the warm room flows into the cold ice cream, turning it from solid to liquid.
  • Your hands warming up a cold object – Your warm hands transfer heat to a cold glass, warming the glass while your hands feel cooler.
  • An oven cooking food – Heat flows from the hot oven to the cold food inside, cooking it.
  • A radiator heating a room – The hot radiator transfers heat to the cooler air, warming up the entire room.

Thermal Equilibrium

When heat flows between two objects long enough, they eventually reach the same temperature. This is called thermal equilibrium. Once this happens, heat stops flowing between them because there's no longer a temperature difference.

If you left a hot cup of tea on the table for hours, what would eventually happen to its temperature? It would become the same temperature as the room! The heat keeps flowing out until the tea and the room are both the same temperature. Then no more heat flows.

Effects of Heat Gain and Loss

What Happens When Objects Gain or Lose Heat?

Heat causes dramatic changes in matter. When objects gain heat (heat goes in), or lose heat (heat goes out), several things can happen:

1. Expansion (Objects Get Bigger)

When most materials gain heat, they expand—they take up more space. The particles inside vibrate faster and move farther apart, making the object slightly larger.

  • Metal expansion – When a metal bar is heated, it becomes longer and wider. This is why metal rulers can give incorrect measurements when hot.
  • Railway tracks – Train tracks have small gaps between sections because the metal expands when heated by the sun. If there were no gaps, the track would buckle on hot days!
  • Mercury thermometers – The mercury inside expands when it gets hot and rises up the tube, showing us the temperature.
  • Bridges and buildings – Engineers leave expansion joints to allow for growth and shrinking with temperature changes.

2. Contraction (Objects Get Smaller)

When objects lose heat, they contract—they become smaller. The opposite of expansion, particles move closer together.

  • Cooling metal – A hot metal wheel shrinks as it cools, which is why wheelwrights (ancient craftspeople) used heat and cooling to fit metal bands around wooden wheels.
  • Shrinking glass – A hot glass bottle will crack if cold water is suddenly poured into it because the inside contracts faster than the outside, creating stress.

3. Change of State (Solid → Liquid → Gas)

Heat can change matter from one state to another:

Process What Happens Heat Added or Removed? Example
Melting Solid → Liquid Heat ADDED Ice cream melts in the sun
Boiling Liquid → Gas Heat ADDED Water boils at 100°C, becoming steam
Evaporation Liquid → Gas (slow) Heat ADDED Wet clothes dry on the line
Freezing Liquid → Solid Heat REMOVED Water freezes into ice at 0°C
Condensation Gas → Liquid Heat REMOVED Steam cools and becomes water droplets
Remember: Melting, boiling, and evaporation all need heat (endothermic). Freezing and condensation release heat (exothermic). Think "Heat In" for melting/boiling, "Heat Out" for freezing/condensing.

4. Temperature Rise and Fall

The most obvious effect of heat is a change in temperature. When heat is added, temperature goes up. When heat is removed, temperature goes down.

  • Thermometer readings – A thermometer shows temperature change as heat is gained or lost.
  • Cooking food – Heat raises the temperature of raw food, cooking it.
  • Cooling beverages – Ice in a drink lowers its temperature as heat transfers to the melting ice.
Some materials expand so much when heated that they can literally bend on their own! Bimetallic strips (two different metals bonded together) curve when heated because one metal expands more than the other. These strips are used in old-fashioned thermostats!
What happens to metal when it's heated?
It expands (gets bigger) because particles vibrate faster and move farther apart.
What's the difference between boiling and evaporation?
Boiling is fast (bubbles throughout liquid at 100°C). Evaporation is slow (surface molecules escape at any temperature).
Why do railway tracks have gaps?
Metal expands in heat. Gaps prevent buckling when tracks heat up in the sun.

Conductors and Insulators of Heat

Good Conductors of Heat

Some materials allow heat to flow through them quickly and easily. These are called good conductors of heat. The champion heat conductors are metals.

Metals: Excellent Heat Conductors

Metals like copper, aluminium, iron, and steel are excellent heat conductors. Heat travels through them very quickly.

  • Copper – The best common conductor. Used in electric wires and cooking pans because heat travels through it so fast.
  • Aluminium – Very light and a good conductor. Used in cookware, radiators, and aircraft.
  • Steel – Strong and conducts heat reasonably well. Used in cooking pots and pans.
  • Iron – A good conductor, used in cookware and industrial equipment.

Why Metals Conduct Heat Well

Metals contain free electrons that can move easily through the material. When one part of metal gets hot, these electrons carry the heat energy rapidly to other parts. It's like a relay race where energy passes from atom to atom at high speed.

When you place a metal spoon in a hot cup of tea, you can feel the handle getting hot very quickly. This is because the metal is an excellent conductor—heat travels up the spoon from the hot water to your hand!

Poor Conductors of Heat (Insulators)

Some materials do NOT allow heat to flow through them easily. These are called poor conductors of heat or insulators. They slow down or stop heat transfer.

Common Insulators

  • Wood – Heat travels through wood very slowly. That's why wooden spoon handles don't get hot.
  • Plastic – Poor conductor, used for pot handles and thermoses.
  • Rubber – Excellent insulator, used for oven mitts and electrical insulation.
  • Cloth/Fabric – Traps air and is a poor conductor, used in blankets and clothing.
  • Air/Gases – Very poor conductor! Air is why thermos bottles have a vacuum or air gap between the walls.
  • Styrofoam – Mostly air, it's an excellent insulator. Used in cooler boxes and packing.
Styrofoam and cork are such good insulators because they're mostly air with tiny pockets. Air is one of the poorest heat conductors we know! That's why winter clothes trap air between layers of fabric to keep you warm.

Why Insulators Don't Conduct Heat Well

Unlike metals with free electrons, insulators don't have electrons that can move freely. Their atoms hold onto their electrons tightly, so heat energy can't travel quickly through them. Heat must move particle by particle, which is much slower.

Section B: Open-Ended Questions

1
A metal spoon and a plastic spoon are placed in a cup of hot water. After 5 minutes, the metal spoon feels much hotter than the plastic spoon. Explain why.

Model Answer:

The metal spoon feels hotter because metal is a good conductor of heat. Heat from the hot water flows quickly through the metal spoon to your hand. The plastic spoon is a poor conductor (insulator), so heat flows through it slowly, and your hand doesn't feel as much heat.

2
Describe what happens to a metal rod when it is heated. Explain why this happens in terms of particles.

Model Answer:

When a metal rod is heated, it expands (gets longer and wider). This happens because the particles in the metal vibrate faster when heated. As they vibrate faster, they move farther apart, taking up more space. Therefore, the entire rod becomes larger.

3
A thermos bottle keeps hot drinks warm for many hours. Explain how the design of a thermos uses the properties of heat conductors and insulators.

Model Answer:

A thermos bottle has double walls with air or vacuum between them. This design uses the fact that air is a very poor conductor of heat. The poor conductor (insulator) prevents heat from flowing out of the hot drink to the outside. The plastic or rubber exterior is also a poor conductor, further slowing heat loss. This combination of insulators keeps the drink hot for a long time.

Key Values in Science

Understanding heat helps us design safer products, use energy efficiently, and care for our environment. By learning how heat flows and choosing the right materials, we can reduce energy waste and make the world more sustainable.
