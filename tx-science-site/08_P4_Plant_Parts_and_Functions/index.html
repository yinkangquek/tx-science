Parts of a Plant: Overview

All plants have basic parts that work together to help the plant survive. Whether it's a tall tree, a small flower, or a vegetable in your garden, most plants have five main parts: roots, stems, leaves, flowers, and fruits.

Each part has a special job. The roots drink water from the soil, the stem carries that water up, the leaves use sunlight to make food, flowers help make new plants, and fruits protect seeds. Let's explore each part in detail.

1. Roots: The Plant's Underground System

What are roots? Roots are the parts of a plant that grow underground. They are usually white, brown, or light colored. Roots do not have leaves or flowers.

Main functions of roots:

  • Absorb water and minerals: Roots drink up water from the soil. This water contains important minerals like nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium that help the plant grow. The roots have tiny hairs called root hairs that increase their surface area to absorb more water.
  • Anchor the plant: Roots hold the plant firmly in the soil so it doesn't blow away in the wind or get pulled out by rain.
  • Store food: Some roots, like carrots and potatoes, store food that the plant makes in its leaves and uses later when it needs energy.

Types of roots:

  • Tap roots: One main thick root that grows straight down deep into the soil, with smaller branches coming off it. Examples: carrots, turnips, and trees like mango and papaya. Tap roots are good at finding water deep underground.
  • Fibrous roots: Many thin roots of equal size spreading out sideways and downward like a mop. Examples: grass, rice, and most small herbaceous plants. Fibrous roots are good at catching water from light rain and holding soil together.
Key Concept The tiny root hairs near the root tip are where most water and minerals are absorbed. These hairs are so small you need a microscope to see them, but they greatly increase the surface area available for absorption.
Did You Know? The roots of a large tree can be as long or longer than its branches! Some roots can go down 10 meters or more into the soil to find water. The root of a plant is always looking for water and minerals in the soil.

2. Stem: The Plant's Transport System

What is a stem? The stem is the part of the plant that grows above the ground. It's usually green, but it can also be brown, red, or other colors. It connects the roots to the leaves, flowers, and fruits, carrying water and minerals upward and food downward.

Main functions of the stem:

  • Transport water and minerals upward: The stem has special tubes called xylem that carry water and minerals from the roots up to the leaves. These tubes are like tiny pipes in a water system.
  • Transport food downward: The stem has other tubes called phloem that carry food made in the leaves down to the roots and other parts of the plant.
  • Support: The stem holds the leaves, flowers, and fruits up toward the sun so they can get as much light as possible.

Types of stems:

  • Herbaceous stems: Soft and green, bending easily, lasting only one growing season. Examples: lettuce, grass, and beans. These plants are called herbs.
  • Woody stems: Hard and brown, lasting for many years, growing larger each year. Examples: tree trunks and shrub stems. The wood is made of special cells that become hard and strong.
Think About It When you cut a stem in half, can you see the tiny tubes inside? In many woody stems, you can see rings in the cross-section. These growth rings show how much the stem grew each year. One ring = one year of growth!

3. Leaves: The Plant's Food Factory

What are leaves? Leaves are the flat, usually green parts attached to the stem. They are the most important parts for making food using sunlight.

Parts of a leaf:

  • Blade: The flat, green part of the leaf where most food-making happens. It's usually larger and thinner to catch more sunlight.
  • Veins: The lines running through the leaf that carry water to the leaf and carry the food made by the leaf to other parts of the plant.
  • Midrib: The thickest vein running down the middle of the leaf. It's like the main highway for transport.
  • Petiole (leaf stalk): The thin stem connecting the leaf to the main stem. It bends and moves the leaf toward the sunlight.

Main function: Photosynthesis (Food Making)

Leaves make food for the plant using three things:

  • Sunlight: Energy from the sun (the power source)
  • Water: Absorbed by roots from soil (brought up by the stem)
  • Carbon dioxide (CO₂): A gas from the air that enters through tiny holes in the leaf (called stomata)

When these three things combine in the leaf, a chemical reaction happens that makes glucose (sugar) which is food for the plant. This process is called photosynthesis. The green color of leaves comes from a special pigment called chlorophyll that captures sunlight. The leaf also releases oxygen as a byproduct of this process, which all animals (including humans) need to breathe!

Gas exchange through stomata: Leaves have tiny holes called stomata (singular: stoma) usually on the bottom surface of the leaf. These holes allow carbon dioxide from the air to enter the leaf. They also allow oxygen made by the plant to leave. Stomata also allow water to leave the plant in a process called transpiration. On hot days, the plant closes its stomata to save water.

Key Concept The equation for photosynthesis is: Sunlight + Water + Carbon Dioxide = Glucose + Oxygen. This happens in the green parts of leaves containing chlorophyll. The glucose is food that gives the plant energy to grow.
Did You Know? If a plant doesn't get enough sunlight, its leaves turn pale yellow instead of green. This is because the plant cannot make enough chlorophyll (the green pigment) without sunlight. Plants are trying to tell you they need more light!

4. Flowers: The Plant's Reproductive System

What are flowers? Flowers are the colorful parts of plants. They are the plant's way of making new plants. Not all plants have flowers, but most plants we know have them.

Main parts of a flower:

  • Petals: The colorful, delicate parts that people love. They attract insects and other animals. Different colors and scents help attract different pollinators.
  • Sepals: Green leaf-like parts that protect the flower before it opens. They wrap around the bud like a coat.
  • Stamen: The male part that produces pollen (a fine yellow powder). The stamen has two parts: the filament (thin stalk) and the anther (the part that makes pollen).
  • Pistil: The female part in the center that receives pollen and develops into a fruit containing seeds. The pistil has three parts: the stigma (sticky top), style (middle tube), and ovary (bottom part that becomes the fruit).

Function of flowers: Flowers are for reproduction. They produce seeds that can grow into new plants. Flowers attract insects, birds, and other animals which help carry pollen from flower to flower. This is called pollination.

Pollination: For a flower to make seeds, pollen from the stamen of one flower must reach the pistil of another flower (or sometimes the same flower). This can happen in two main ways:

  • Insect pollination: Bees, butterflies, moths, and other insects visit flowers to drink nectar (a sweet liquid) and collect pollen for food. As they move from flower to flower, pollen sticks to their bodies and gets transferred to other flowers.
  • Wind pollination: Some plants, like grasses and many trees, rely on wind to carry pollen from flower to flower. These flowers are often plain and small with no smell.
  • Water pollination: Some water plants use water to carry pollen.
Think About It Why do you think flowers are often colorful and smell sweet? The colors and scents help attract insects! This is an adaptation that helps the plant reproduce. Nature has designed a perfect partnership between flowers and insects.

5. Fruits: Protecting and Dispersing Seeds

What are fruits? A fruit is the part of a plant that develops from a flower after pollination and fertilization. It contains seeds inside, and the fruit's job is to protect the seeds and help spread them to new places.

Main function of fruits:

  • Protect seeds: The fruit's hard or thick outer layer protects the delicate seeds inside from damage, drying out, or being eaten before they're ready.
  • Help disperse seeds: Fruits help spread seeds to new places where they can grow into new plants. This spreads the plant to different areas.

How seeds are dispersed (spread):

  • By animals: Many fruits are bright colored and taste good, so animals eat them. The seeds pass through the animal's body and come out in their droppings far away from the parent plant. Examples: mangoes, apples, tomatoes, berries. This is good for the plant because it spreads seeds to new areas.
  • By wind: Some fruits are very light with wings or hairy/fluffy parts that catch the wind and carry the seeds away. Examples: maple seeds (helicopter seeds), dandelion seeds, pine seeds. These sail through the air like parachutes.
  • By water: Some fruits can float and are carried by rivers, ocean currents, and floods. Examples: coconut (can float on ocean water for months). The seeds can travel very far by water.
  • By explosion: Some fruit pods dry out and split open forcefully, throwing seeds far away from the plant. Examples: pea pods, beans, touch-me-nots. The seeds can be thrown several meters away!
Did You Know? Many things we eat are actually fruits! Tomatoes, beans, peas, and peppers are all fruits because they develop from flowers and contain seeds. People often think of fruits as only sweet things like apples or oranges, but in science, any seed-containing structure that develops from a flower is a fruit.

Structure and Function Relationships

Scientists notice that the shape and structure of plant parts match what they do. This is called adaptation. When the structure (shape and form) is perfectly designed for the function (job it does), we know nature has designed it well:

  • Roots are underground: This protects them from damage and keeps them in the cool, moist soil where water is available.
  • Leaves are flat: This gives a large surface area to catch as much sunlight as possible for photosynthesis.
  • Leaves are thin: This allows light to penetrate deep into the leaf and makes it easier for gases to reach all cells inside.
  • Roots have root hairs: These tiny extensions greatly increase surface area to absorb more water and minerals.
  • Stems are strong and thick: They need to be strong to support the weight of leaves, flowers, and fruits against gravity.
  • Flowers are colorful and scented: This helps attract pollinators like bees, butterflies, and birds.
  • Fruits are colorful: This helps attract animals to eat them and disperse seeds.
  • Some fruits have wings or fluffy structures: These are adapted for wind dispersal, like maple seeds.
Key Concept Structure follows function. The way a plant part looks and is built tells us what job it does. Plants are perfectly designed by millions of years of evolution to survive and reproduce in their environment.

PSLE Practice Questions

Section A: Multiple Choice Questions (MCQ)
1. Which of the following is a function of the stem?
Correct Answer: B

The stem transports water and minerals absorbed by roots to all parts of the plant. It also provides support so leaves can get sunlight and flowers/fruits can be displayed.

2. What do roots have that increases their ability to absorb water and minerals?
Correct Answer: A

Root hairs are tiny structures on roots that greatly increase the surface area. This larger surface area allows roots to absorb more water and dissolved minerals from the soil.

3. Which part of the plant makes food?
Correct Answer: C

Leaves make food through photosynthesis using sunlight, water, and carbon dioxide. This food (glucose) is used by the plant for energy and growth.

4. What is the main purpose of a flower?
Correct Answer: B

Flowers are the reproductive parts of plants. After pollination, flowers develop into fruits containing seeds that can grow into new plants.

5. Which of the following is NOT a way seeds are dispersed?
Correct Answer: C

Roots absorb water and minerals and anchor plants. They do not disperse seeds. Seeds are dispersed by animals, wind, water, and sometimes by explosion of fruit pods.

Section B: Open-Ended Questions
1. A student removed all the leaves from Plant A but left Plant B with all its leaves intact. After one week, Plant A became wilted and unhealthy. Explain why removing the leaves caused Plant A to wilt.
Model Answer

Explanation:

Leaves have two main functions related to this outcome:

  • Making food (photosynthesis): Leaves make food for the plant using sunlight. Without leaves, Plant A cannot make food, so it becomes weak and unhealthy because it has no energy.
  • Transport of water and nutrients: Leaves play a role in the plant's water balance. Without leaves, water that is absorbed by roots cannot be properly used and transported throughout the plant.

Conclusion: Without leaves, Plant A cannot make the food (glucose) it needs to stay healthy and strong. This energy loss causes the cells to become weak and unable to maintain their rigidity, so the plant wilts.

2. Look at the diagram below showing three different root systems. Identify which is a tap root system and which are fibrous root systems. Explain one advantage of each type of root system.
Model Answer

Identification:

  • Tap root: Has one main root with smaller roots branching off (example: carrot root system)
  • Fibrous roots: Has many equal-sized roots spreading out like a mop (example: grass root system)

Advantages:

  • Tap root advantage: Can grow deep into the soil to reach water sources far below the surface, useful in dry areas or for trees that need deep anchoring
  • Fibrous root advantage: Spreads widely near the surface to catch water from light rain or soil moisture, and the wide spread holds soil together to prevent erosion
3. A flower has petals, sepals, stamens (male parts), and pistils (female parts). Explain how the structure of each of these parts relates to its function in helping the plant reproduce.
Model Answer

Structure-Function Relationships:

  • Petals (structure): Colorful and fragrant. (function): Attract insects and animals which carry pollen between flowers (pollination)
  • Sepals (structure): Green and leaf-like, protective layers. (function): Protect the delicate flower parts before the flower opens
  • Stamens (structure): Produce pollen (yellow powder). (function): Male reproductive part that produces pollen containing male cells for reproduction
  • Pistil (structure): Located in center with sticky stigma at top. (function): Female reproductive part that receives pollen and develops into fruit containing seeds

Conclusion: All these structures work together to ensure pollination occurs and seeds develop, allowing the plant to reproduce successfully.
