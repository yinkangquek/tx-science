⚙️ The Respiratory System

What is the Respiratory System?

The respiratory system is a group of organs that helps your body take in oxygen from the air and get rid of carbon dioxide (a waste gas). Every time you breathe, you are using your respiratory system to keep your body healthy and your cells alive.

Key Concept: Breathing brings fresh oxygen into your body and removes waste carbon dioxide. This happens automatically, but you can also control your breathing when needed.

Parts of the Respiratory System

🫁 The Respiratory System

Air travels through the nose → trachea → bronchi → lungs → alveoli, where oxygen enters the blood!

Part Location Function
Nose Front of face Filters dust and germs from air; warms and moistens the air before it enters the lungs
Windpipe (Trachea) In the neck A tube that carries air from the nose down to the lungs; has rings of cartilage to keep it open
Lungs Inside the chest cavity Spongy organs where oxygen enters the blood and carbon dioxide leaves the blood; there are two lungs
Diaphragm Below the lungs A muscle that contracts and relaxes to help pull air in and push air out of the lungs
Did You Know? Your lungs are covered with tiny air sacs called alveoli. There are about 300 million of them! This huge surface area allows oxygen and carbon dioxide to be exchanged efficiently.

How Breathing Works: Inhalation vs Exhalation

Inhalation (Breathing In):

  • The diaphragm contracts (tightens) and moves downward
  • The ribcage expands (gets larger) and moves upward and outward
  • This makes the space inside the lungs bigger
  • Air rushes in through your nose into the lungs to fill the space

Exhalation (Breathing Out):

  • The diaphragm relaxes and moves upward
  • The ribcage contracts (gets smaller) and moves downward and inward
  • This makes the space inside the lungs smaller
  • Air is pushed out of the lungs through your nose or mouth
  • This air contains waste carbon dioxide that your body wants to get rid of
Think About It: Try this activity: Place your hand on your ribcage. Now take a deep breath in. Can you feel your ribcage expand? When you breathe out, you should feel it contract. Your diaphragm is moving too, but it's hidden inside your body!

Gas Exchange in the Lungs

The key job of the lungs is to swap gases between the air you breathe and your blood. Inside the lungs:

  • Oxygen (O₂) from the air in your lungs passes through the thin walls of the alveoli into tiny blood vessels called capillaries
  • Carbon dioxide (CO₂) waste from your blood passes through the capillary walls into the alveoli so it can be breathed out
  • This exchange happens continuously, even when you're sleeping
Key Concept: Gas exchange is like a trade: your blood gives carbon dioxide to the lungs, and the lungs give oxygen to your blood in return.

Heart: The Circulatory System

What is the Circulatory System?

The circulatory system is made up of your heart, blood vessels, and blood. It works like a delivery network for your body. It carries oxygen and nutrients to all your cells and picks up waste products to get rid of them. Your circulatory system never stops working—it keeps running even while you sleep!

Key Concept: Your circulatory system is your body's transportation system. The heart is the engine, blood vessels are the roads, and blood is the delivery truck.

Parts of the Circulatory System

❤️ Heart and Blood Circulation

The heart pumps oxygenated blood (red) to the body and deoxygenated blood (blue) to the lungs. This cycle repeats about 60-100 times every minute!

Part Type & Location Function
Heart Muscular organ, left side of chest Pumps blood to all parts of the body; beats about 70 times per minute at rest (faster after exercise)
Arteries Blood vessels with thick, muscular walls Carry oxygen-rich blood away from the heart to all body cells
Veins Blood vessels with thinner walls Carry oxygen-poor blood and waste products back to the heart
Capillaries Tiny blood vessels, just one cell thick Allow oxygen, nutrients, and waste to be exchanged between blood and body cells
Blood Red liquid tissue Carries oxygen and nutrients; removes waste carbon dioxide; fights infections; helps blood clotting
Did You Know? If you stretched out all the blood vessels in your body, they would wrap around Earth about 2.5 times! There are about 60,000 miles of blood vessels in an adult human body.

The Heart: Your Body's Pump

The heart is about the size of your fist and is one of the most important organs in your body. Here's what makes it special:

  • It's a muscular organ that pumps blood continuously
  • A healthy heart at rest beats about 60-70 times per minute
  • After exercise, your heart rate speeds up to pump more oxygenated blood to working muscles
  • The left side of the heart pumps oxygen-rich blood to the body; the right side pumps oxygen-poor blood to the lungs
  • You can feel your heart beating by taking your pulse at your wrist or neck
Think About It: Place two fingers gently on the side of your neck (just below your jaw) or on the inside of your wrist. You should feel your pulse—the rhythmic beating of your heart pumping blood through the arteries. Count how many beats you feel in 60 seconds. That's your resting heart rate!

Blood: The Delivery Liquid

Blood is a liquid tissue that does much more than just flow through your body. It has several important jobs:

  • Transports oxygen: Picks up oxygen from the lungs and delivers it to every cell in your body
  • Transports nutrients: Carries food nutrients from your digestive system to body cells for energy
  • Removes waste: Picks up carbon dioxide from cells and carries it to the lungs to be breathed out
  • Fights infection: Contains white blood cells that defend your body against germs
  • Helps healing: When you get a cut, blood clots to stop the bleeding and platelets help it heal
  • Regulates temperature: Blood helps keep your body at the right temperature
Key Concept: Oxygenated blood is bright red. Deoxygenated blood is a darker, purplish-red colour. Veins appear blue through the skin, but the blood inside is always a shade of red — never truly blue.

Cycling: How They Work Together

The Connection Between Two Systems

The respiratory and circulatory systems are deeply connected. They work together like teammates to keep your body healthy and full of energy. Neither system can do its job properly without the other.

Key Concept: The respiratory system brings oxygen into your body, and the circulatory system delivers that oxygen to every cell. They work in perfect harmony.

The Journey of Oxygen: Step by Step

Follow the complete path that oxygen takes through your body:

  1. Inhalation: You breathe in air through your nose or mouth
  2. To the lungs: Air travels down the windpipe (trachea) into your lungs
  3. Gas exchange in lungs: Oxygen from the air passes through the thin alveoli walls into the capillaries in your lungs
  4. Heart receives oxygen-rich blood: Blood carrying fresh oxygen returns to the right side of the heart from the lungs
  5. Heart pumps out: The heart pumps this oxygen-rich blood out through arteries to all parts of your body
  6. Oxygen to cells: As blood travels through capillaries, oxygen passes out into the cells around them
  7. Cells use oxygen: Your cells use oxygen for energy, creating carbon dioxide as a waste product
  8. Blood picks up CO₂: Blood in the capillaries picks up carbon dioxide from the cells
  9. Blood returns to heart: Oxygen-poor blood travels back to the heart through the veins
  10. To the lungs: The heart pumps this oxygen-poor blood to the lungs
  11. Gas exchange again: Carbon dioxide leaves the blood and enters the alveoli in the lungs
  12. Exhalation: You breathe out, getting rid of the carbon dioxide waste
  13. Cycle repeats: The whole process starts again!

What Happens When You Exercise?

Your muscles need extra oxygen when you're active. Watch what happens:

At Rest During Exercise
Heart beats 60-70 times per minute Heart beats 100+ times per minute
You breathe 12-20 times per minute You breathe 30+ times per minute
Breathing is shallow and calm Breathing is deep and fast
Less blood flows to muscles More blood flows to active muscles
Muscles produce little waste CO₂ Muscles produce lots of waste CO₂
Did You Know? During intense exercise, your breathing can increase from 12 breaths per minute to over 60 breaths per minute! This allows your lungs to pull in more oxygen and remove more carbon dioxide waste.

Why Both Systems Must Work Together

If either system stopped working, your body would be in serious trouble:

  • Without breathing: Oxygen wouldn't enter your blood, and carbon dioxide wouldn't be removed. Your cells would quickly run out of energy and die.
  • Without circulation: Oxygen would enter your lungs but wouldn't reach your cells. It would be like filling a gas tank but not being able to use the fuel.
  • Together: The respiratory system is the supplier, and the circulatory system is the delivery driver. They create a perfect teamwork system!
Think About It: Your body is like a business. The respiratory system is the supplier that gets raw materials (oxygen), and the circulatory system is the delivery company that takes those materials to all the different departments (cells). Without either one, the business falls apart!

PSLE-Style Practice Questions

Section A: Multiple Choice Questions (1 mark each)

Q1.
Which part of the respiratory system filters and warms air before it enters the lungs?
Correct Answer: B
The nose is responsible for filtering dust and germs from the air, and warming and moistening it before it enters the lungs.
Q2.
What happens to the diaphragm when you inhale (breathe in)?
Correct Answer: B
During inhalation, the diaphragm contracts (tightens) and moves downward. This increases the space in the lungs, causing air to rush in.
Q3.
Which type of blood vessel carries oxygen-poor blood back to the heart?
Correct Answer: B
Veins carry oxygen-poor blood and waste products back to the heart. Arteries carry oxygen-rich blood away from the heart.
Q4.
Where does gas exchange (oxygen in, carbon dioxide out) happen in the lungs?
Correct Answer: B
Gas exchange happens in the alveoli—tiny air sacs in the lungs. Oxygen passes into the blood, and carbon dioxide passes out.
Q5.
What is the main job of the heart?
Correct Answer: C
The heart is a muscular organ that pumps blood throughout the body, delivering oxygen and nutrients to all cells.

Section B: Open-Ended Questions (2-3 marks each)

Q6.
Describe what happens to your breathing and heart rate when you run. Why do these changes occur? (2 marks)
Model Answer (2 marks):
When you run, your breathing becomes faster and deeper, and your heart rate increases (beats faster).

Why this happens: Your muscles are working hard and need more oxygen. Your respiratory system breathes faster to get more oxygen into your blood, and your circulatory system pumps faster to deliver this oxygen-rich blood to your muscles quickly. Your muscles also produce more carbon dioxide waste, which your systems must remove faster.
Q7.
Explain how oxygen travels from the air you breathe to a muscle cell in your leg. Name at least 4 body parts in your answer. (3 marks)
Model Answer (3 marks):
1. Air enters your nose, which filters and warms it. (1 mark)

2. Air travels down the windpipe (trachea) into the lungs. (1 mark)

3. In the lungs, oxygen passes through the alveoli into tiny blood vessels (capillaries). The blood carries oxygen to the heart, which pumps it out through arteries. (1 mark)

4. The arteries branch into capillaries around your leg muscle cells, where oxygen passes out of the blood into the muscle cells.
Q8.
After running, Mei noticed she was breathing faster and her heart was beating faster. Explain why both the respiratory and circulatory systems had to work harder. (3 marks)
Model Answer (3 marks):
Why breathing became faster: Mei's muscles were working hard and needed much more oxygen. Her respiratory system had to breathe faster to pull in more oxygen from the air into her lungs. She also produced more carbon dioxide waste that needed to be removed. (1 mark)

Why her heart beat faster: Her heart had to pump faster to deliver the extra oxygen-rich blood to her hardworking muscles quickly. It also had to pick up carbon dioxide waste from the muscles and carry it back to the lungs. (1 mark)

How they work together: The respiratory and circulatory systems work as a team. The faster breathing gets more oxygen into the blood, and the faster heartbeat delivers this oxygen to the muscles that need it. Without both systems working harder together, her muscles wouldn't get enough oxygen. (1 mark)
